The Grand Riddle Faire

~8 minutes · Princess Savannah, Luna, Finnegan the Fox, Mossy the Turtle, Wilma, Bramford the Badger

Once upon a time, in a kingdom tucked between a great green forest and a range of misty mountains, there was one day each year that everyone looked forward to more than any other.

It was the day of the Grand Riddle Faire.

Every spring, when the wildflowers opened and the breeze smelled like honey, the kingdom's Great Green was transformed into the most wonderful faire you ever saw. There were colorful tents with striped tops. There were wooden stages stacked high with puzzle-boxes. There were winding hedge-mazes with paths that looped and doubled back. And from every post and pole, bright banners fluttered in the wind.

But the most wonderful thing of all stood right in the center.

The Riddle Tower.

It was an old stone tower, covered in soft green moss, with a winding staircase and a single round room at the top. And in that room was a puzzle that no one had solved in a hundred years.

Princess Savannah loved riddles.

Every night before bed, she and Luna played guessing games in the dark, and Luna would bark out wrong answers on purpose — louder and wronger each time — just to make Savannah laugh.

"Is it a shoe?" Luna would roar.

"No, Luna."

"Is it a BIGGER shoe?"

"That's not even a real guess!"

"Is it a shoe that's also a HAT?"

And Savannah would laugh until her sides hurt, and Luna would look very pleased with herself, and that was how every night ended.

This year, for the very first time, Savannah had decided to enter the competition.

She walked through the meadow that morning with Luna at her side, watching the workers string up the last of the lanterns. Finnegan the Fox was already there, of course — darting between booths, sniffing at puzzle-boxes, trying to peek at the riddles early.

"I've almost figured out round one," Finnegan said, tapping his nose. "I have a system."

"You don't have a system," said Mossy the Turtle, who was sitting on a stump near the entrance, slowly reading the rules for the third time. "You have enthusiasm. Those are different things."

"Enthusiasm IS a system," said Finnegan. But he didn't sound very sure.

Savannah signed her name on the big chalkboard where all the competitors' names were written. Her handwriting was small and neat, right at the bottom of the list. The crowd murmured when they saw it. She was the youngest competitor by far.

And one competitor in particular looked down at her name and scoffed.

Bramford the Badger.

He was tall and serious, with silver-streaked fur. He'd won the Grand Riddle Faire three years running, and he walked through the Great Green like the whole thing belonged to him.

"A bit young for this, aren't you?" he said, not unkindly — but not kindly, either.

Luna growled. A low, rumbling growl that made the chalkboard rattle.

"Easy, Luna," said Savannah, resting her hand on the dragon's warm neck.

Finnegan leaned over and whispered, "Bramford's not mean, exactly. He's just so focused on winning that he's forgotten how to have fun."

Mossy nodded slowly. "Being nervous just means you care about something. That's not a bad thing."

Savannah took a deep breath. She wasn't entering because she needed to win. She was entering because riddles were fun, and she wanted to see how far she could go.

Just then, Wilma appeared at the edge of the Faire.

She moved the way she always did — unhurried, elegant, as if the whole world had agreed to wait for her.

She knelt beside Savannah and said, quietly: "The best puzzle-solvers aren't the fastest, dear. They're the ones who look at things from more than one side."

Savannah hugged her tight and whispered, "I'll remember."

And the Faire began.

Round one was the Maze-Boxes.

Each competitor was given a wooden box covered in sliding panels, hidden latches, and false bottoms. Somewhere inside was a single glass marble. Find the marble and you moved on.

The other competitors grabbed their boxes and started pulling and twisting and shaking. Bramford worked his with quick, practiced hands.

Savannah picked hers up and turned it over.

She looked at it from the top. She looked at it from the bottom. She looked at it from every side, the way Wilma had said. And there, on the underside, she felt a tiny latch that didn't match the others. She pressed it, and a hidden drawer slid open, and inside was a marble — deep blue, like a little piece of the sky.

"She got it!" Finnegan yelled from the crowd, jumping so high he nearly tripped over his own tail.

Luna roared. Not a scary roar — a happy one, so loud that the banners flapped and two puzzle-boxes fell off a table.

Several competitors were eliminated. But Savannah moved on.

Round two was the Riddle-Gates.

A row of tall wooden gates stretched across the meadow, each one carved with a riddle in curling letters. You could only pass through a gate if you answered its riddle correctly. Get it wrong, and the gate stayed shut.

Savannah walked to the first gate and read:

I have hands but cannot clap. I have a face but cannot smile. What am I?

"A clock," said Savannah.

The gate swung open.

She walked to the second:

The more you take, the more you leave behind. What am I?

Savannah paused. She thought. She smiled.

"Footsteps."

The gate swung open.

From the crowd, Finnegan shouted, "It's a sandwich!"

He clapped his paw over his mouth. "Sorry! I panicked!"

Mossy shook his head very slowly.

Luna bounced up and down on the sidelines so hard the ground shook, and a nearby lemonade stand wobbled dangerously.

Gate by gate, riddle by riddle, Savannah made her way through. Sometimes she paused. Sometimes she laughed when she caught the trick. But she kept going, steady and sure, and one by one the gates swung open for her.

By the end of round two, only two competitors remained.

Savannah and Bramford.

The crowd went quiet. The lanterns swayed. The breeze held its breath.

It was time for the Riddle Tower.

Savannah and Bramford climbed the winding stone steps together, side by side but not speaking. Luna pressed her nose against the tower window from outside, watching. Finnegan stood on Mossy's shell to get a better view, and Mossy — for once — didn't seem to mind.

Inside the tower room, the final puzzle was carved into the stone floor.

It was a huge circle, split down the middle into two halves. Each half was covered in strange, beautiful symbols — swirls and stars and shapes that almost looked like letters. On opposite sides of the room, two sets of stone handles jutted from the wall.

Savannah studied the floor. She walked around the circle once, twice, then stopped.

And she understood.

The symbols on her side were the answers to the symbols on his side. And his symbols were the answers to hers. Each half of the circle needed the other half to make sense.

It wasn't a puzzle for one person.

It was a puzzle for two.

Bramford was already at his handles, pulling hard, trying to force the stone to move by strength alone. He'd been trying to solve this puzzle by himself for three years. Every year he climbed these stairs. Every year the stone stayed still.

Savannah could have stayed on her side. She could have waited for him to fail.

But that wasn't how the puzzle worked. And it wasn't who she was.

She walked across the room.

"I think we're supposed to do this together," she said.

Bramford looked at her. His hands were still on the handles. His eyes were tired.

"Together?" he said, like it was a word he'd forgotten.

Savannah didn't push. She didn't argue. She just looked at the symbols on his side of the circle and started reading them out loud.

"The swirl with three points — I think that matches the open star on my side. The answer is moonrise."

Bramford blinked. He looked at her symbols. Slowly, carefully, he read one back.

"The double arch — that matches your wave pattern. The answer is... bridge."

"Yes!" said Savannah.

They called answers back and forth across the circle. Savannah read his symbols. Bramford read hers. One by one, the answers came — some quickly, some after a long pause, some after they both started laughing at the same wrong guess.

From outside the tower window, Luna barked encouragement. Loud, booming barks that rattled the old stones.

"You've GOT this!" she roared.

The stone halves began to turn.

Click.

Click.

Click.

Slowly, the two halves of the circle moved toward each other, symbols lining up like pieces of a song finding their melody.

Click.

And when the last piece locked into place, the tower filled with warm golden light — soft and steady, like sunrise, like coming home.

The circle opened to reveal a small wooden chest, old and beautiful. Savannah lifted the lid.

Inside were two silver pins.

Each one was shaped like half of a puzzle piece, and when you held them side by side, they fit together perfectly.

There was never meant to be one winner. The riddle's answer, all along, was partnership.

Savannah held out one pin to Bramford. He took it. And for the first time all day, he smiled — a real smile, warm and wide.

"Thank you," he said quietly. "I forgot that puzzles are supposed to be fun."

"They're the most fun," said Savannah, "when you have someone to solve them with."

They walked out of the Riddle Tower together, side by side, each wearing a silver pin that caught the afternoon light. And when the crowd saw them — both of them, together — they erupted.

Cheering. Clapping. Stamping their feet so hard the Great Green trembled.

Finnegan pushed through the crowd, tail high. "I knew it!" he announced. "I knew the answer the whole time! It was teamwork! I was going to say teamwork!"

"You said it was a sandwich," said Mossy.

"That was ALSO a valid answer."

Mossy blinked slowly. "Some things," he said, in his calm, steady way, "are worth being slow about."

Luna didn't wait for the crowd to clear. She barreled through them like a black-scaled boulder, knocked Savannah clean off her feet, and tackled her into the grass in a hug so big and so enthusiastic that they rolled three full times before stopping.

"You did it! You did it! You did it!" Luna roared, her tail thumping the ground like a drum.

"WE did it," Savannah laughed, grass in her hair and the silver pin still shining on her chest.

From the edge of the crowd, Wilma caught Savannah's eye and gave a small, knowing nod — the kind that said I knew you would and I'm so proud, all without a single word.

The Faire went on into the evening. Lanterns glowed like earthbound stars. Friends shared riddles just for the joy of it — not to win, not to prove anything, just because riddles were fun and sharing them was better.

Bramford stayed the whole night. He laughed at Finnegan's jokes. He let Luna knock him over once, which Luna considered a great honor.

And when the stars came out and the lanterns burned low, Savannah walked home through the meadow with Luna warm at her side.

She set the silver pin on her nightstand, right where the moonlight could find it. Luna curled up at the foot of the bed, her great dark head resting on her paws, already half-dreaming.

"Goodnight, Luna," Savannah whispered.

Luna purred — a deep, rumbling sound, like the tower's stones clicking into place.

And Savannah closed her eyes, and dreamed of next year's puzzles, and the friends she'd solve them with.

And they all lived happily ever after.
