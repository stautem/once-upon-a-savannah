The Colors That Went Missing

~7 minutes · Princess Savannah, Luna, Wilma, Clover the Deer, Petal the Chameleon

Listen to this story:

Once upon a time, in a kingdom tucked between a great green forest and a range of misty mountains, Princess Savannah woke up early.

She always woke up early. She liked to sit at the tall window in her tower room and watch the sunrise paint the sky. First it would go pink. Then gold. Then the softest, warmest blue you ever saw.

Today she had her paints out and ready. Her brushes were lined up. Her water cup was full. Today, she was going to paint the sunrise.

Luna was curled at the foot of the bed, snoring. She was a great black dragon with shining scales and a long tail and wings that folded neatly against her back, and she snored like a very small thunderstorm.

Savannah smiled and looked out the window.

But something was wrong.

The sunrise wasn't there.

The sky wasn't pink. It wasn't gold. It wasn't blue. It was white — not cloudy-white, just... empty-white. Like someone had taken an eraser to the whole sky and rubbed it clean.

Savannah looked down at the meadow. The wildflowers were gray. The trees were gray. Even the river looked pale and thin.

"Luna," Savannah whispered. "Luna, wake up."

Luna opened one eye. Then the other. She yawned a great big yawn, stretched her wings, and looked down at her own paws.

She froze.

Her beautiful black scales had gone dull and ashy — like charcoal left out in the rain. Not gleaming. Not shining. Just... gray.

Luna let out a bark so loud it rattled the window. She shook herself from nose to tail, as if she could shake the gray right off. She could not.

Luna had opinions about many things, and "gray" was not one she approved of.

"I know," said Savannah, kneeling beside her. "I know. We'll fix it."

She reached for her paint pots — but every single one was empty. Not dried up. Not spilled. Just... gone. The whole kingdom had gone gray overnight.

But Savannah wasn't frightened. She was determined. Colors don't just disappear. They have to go somewhere.

And there — right there on her windowsill, bright as a berry — was a tiny drip of blue. On the stone stairs, a drop of gold. On the garden gate, a smudge of pink.

A trail.

"Come on, Luna," said Savannah. She grabbed her empty paint satchel, slung it over her shoulder, and headed for the door. "We're going to follow the colors."

Luna trotted beside her, nose to the ground, sniffing each bright drop. She sneezed at the pink ones — dramatically, every time — because Luna never did anything once when she could do it three times.

The trail led out of the castle, through the meadow, and toward the forest. The drops were small and far apart — a drip here, a smudge there — like breadcrumbs left behind by someone who didn't mean to leave them.

At the tree line, they found Clover.

Clover the Deer was standing very still among the gray trees. Her coat, usually the warm brown of autumn leaves, had turned silver.

Clover didn't say much. She never did. But she fell into step beside Savannah — quiet and steady, the way Clover always was.

So the three of them walked together. Savannah in front, Luna sniffing and sneezing beside her, and Clover stepping softly behind.

The forest was strange without color. The shadows were pale. The moss was gray. Even the songbirds sounded quieter, as if they weren't sure what to sing about when everything looked the same.

But the trail kept going, and the farther they walked, the brighter it got. More drops. Closer together. Richer colors. Whatever had taken them was leaving them behind, little by little, like a cup that was too full.

They came around a bend in the path, and there was Wilma's cottage.

It sat among the trees the way it always did — small and warm, with smoke curling from the chimney. Except today the flowers were gray, and the only thing that looked right was Wilma herself.

She was standing in her garden, calm as always. Her silver hair shone in the pale light — the one thing in the kingdom that was already silver and didn't mind.

"Good morning, Savannah," said Wilma, as if she'd been expecting her.

"The colors are gone," said Savannah.

"I know," said Wilma. "I felt them slip away in the night. Like a song fading out — note by note, until the melody was gone."

"Who took them?"

Wilma tilted her head. "No one took them with meanness," she said. "They were taken with loneliness. That's a different thing entirely."

She placed a small, empty glass jar in Savannah's hands.

"When you find where the colors went, you won't need to take them back. You'll just need to help them feel safe enough to come out."

Savannah tucked the jar into her satchel. Wilma smiled — that smile that made you feel like everything was going to be all right. "You'll do just fine," she said. "You always do."

So Savannah pressed on. The trail grew brighter and brighter. The drops became puddles. The puddles became streaks — sunset orange and forest green and purple as deep as the night sky.

And then the trail ended.

They stood at the edge of a mossy hollow beneath a great old oak tree. The moss was thick and soft, and there — curled into the tightest, smallest ball — was a tiny chameleon. No bigger than Savannah's hand.

But she was blazing.

Every missing color was swirling across her tiny body — pinks and golds and blues and greens and sunset oranges, shifting and rippling like light on water. She looked like a living sunrise.

And she was trembling.

When she saw Savannah, she squeezed herself tighter, and the colors pulsed brighter, as if they were trying to burst out of her all at once.

Savannah knelt down, slowly.

"Oh," she said softly. "It's you."

Luna leaned in and sniffed loudly. The chameleon flinched.

"Easy, Luna," Savannah murmured.

This was Petal. She was a chameleon who changed color with her feelings. She had been so afraid of being seen. So afraid of being noticed. She had curled up tight and tried to disappear. But the harder she tried to hide, the more color she pulled in.

She hadn't meant to. She was just scared.

Savannah didn't scold her. She didn't reach out and grab.

She just sat down. Right there in the moss, cross-legged, the way you sit when you're planning to stay awhile.

"Hello," she said gently. "My name is Savannah."

Petal peeked out a little more. The colors on her skin shifted — nervous oranges, worried purples.

"Your colors are beautiful," said Savannah. "Did you know that? Every single one of them."

Petal blinked. No one had ever said that before.

Luna flopped down on the moss with a great big huff and nuzzled Petal with her big scaly nose. It was Luna's way of saying: You're fine. Stop worrying.

Clover lay down nearby, calm and patient, just being there the way Clover always was.

"I bet you didn't mean to take those colors," said Savannah. "I bet you were just trying to be quiet. To be small. To not bother anyone."

Petal's tiny body shivered. The colors rippled.

"But you're not a bother," said Savannah. "You're not too much. And you don't have to disappear."

She held out her hand, palm up.

"Would you like to come see the kingdom? Not to give the colors back. Just to see it."

For a long, quiet moment, nothing happened.

Then, slowly — so slowly — Petal uncurled.

One tiny foot. Then another. Then her whole small self, stretching out like a flower opening in the morning.

She climbed onto Savannah's hand.

And as she uncurled — as she stopped squeezing, stopped hiding, stopped trying to make herself nothing — the colors began to lift from her skin.

They drifted off like dandelion seeds.

Pink floated up, and the sky blushed. Gold settled down, and the sunlight warmed. Green drifted low, and the grass woke up. Blue slipped away, and the river sparkled.

One by one, the colors returned to where they belonged. Not because anyone forced them. But because Petal wasn't trying to hide anymore.

She sat in Savannah's palm, still colorful — but gently now. A soft rose. A quiet violet. The palest, happiest green. Her own colors. The colors she was always meant to have.

"There you are," Savannah whispered. "There's the real you."

They walked back through the forest, and the world bloomed around them with every step. The trees turned green. The wildflowers lit up in reds and yellows and purples. The songbirds started up again, bright and loud, as if they'd been waiting for permission.

Luna pranced ahead, her scales black and gleaming again. She held her head very high. She was extremely pleased.

Clover's coat warmed back to autumn brown, and she walked a little taller.

When they reached the edge of the meadow, Wilma was waiting.

She stood in the morning light, smiling that smile — the one that said she'd known how this would end.

"You found her," Wilma said.

"She found us," said Savannah, and held up her hand so Wilma could see Petal, who was shifting from rose to gold in the warm light.

"Hello, little one," said Wilma, and her voice was so gentle that Petal turned the softest shade of green.

The sunrise came at last. It was late — very late — but it came in so bright and so pink and so gold that the whole kingdom stopped to look.

Savannah set Petal on Luna's broad nose.

Petal decided she quite liked the view up there. She settled in, tiny and content, her colors shifting freely — rose, then violet, then a warm, sunset orange.

Luna went cross-eyed trying to look at her.

Then Luna sneezed.

It was a tremendous sneeze — the kind that shakes the leaves off trees and startles birds three meadows over. Petal flew up into the air, spun twice, and landed right back on Luna's nose, holding on with all four tiny feet.

Everyone laughed. Even Clover let out a soft huff that was her way of laughing.

Then Savannah picked up her paints — every pot full again, brimming with color. She sat down in the meadow with Luna beside her and Clover nearby. And Petal rode on Luna's nose like a very small captain of a very large, very grumpy ship.

And she painted the sunrise.

She painted the pinks and the golds and the blues. She painted the green meadow and the dark, shining dragon and the deer standing quietly at the edge. And right in the middle, she painted a tiny, bright chameleon, no bigger than a thumbprint, glowing with every color in the world.

And Petal — the smallest, shyest creature in the whole kingdom — never disappeared again. She didn't need to. She had friends now. And that was more than enough.

And they all lived happily ever after.
