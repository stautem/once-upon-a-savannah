Luna the Dragon

~7 minutes · Princess Savannah, Luna, Big Al, Wilma, Ollie the Opossum, Rascal the Raccoon, Bramble the Bat

Listen to this story:

Once upon a time, in a kingdom tucked between a great green forest and a range of misty mountains, there lived a princess named Savannah.

Princess Savannah lived in a cozy castle with tall windows and a garden full of wildflowers. She wasn't the kind of princess who sat around waiting for things to happen. She was the kind of princess who went looking for them.

Every morning, she'd lace up her boots and head into the forest to meet her three best friends.

There was Ollie the Opossum, who was small and gray and very good at pretending to be asleep. There was Rascal the Raccoon, who was clever and quick and always finding things in the stream. And there was Bramble the Bat, who was soft and dark and knew every path through the trees, even in the dark.

Together, they explored the forest, picked berries by the creek, and told each other stories in the meadow while the sun moved slowly across the sky.

It was a very good life.

But one morning, something was different.

The ground rumbled.

Not a big rumble — a small one, like faraway thunder on a sunny day. The birds in the northern woods went quiet. A puff of gray smoke rose above the treetops, far away, near the mountains.

"What was that?" said Rascal, standing on his back legs to get a better look.

Ollie immediately fell over and played dead.

"It came from the mountains," said Bramble, tilting her ears toward the sound. "Something is up there."

The kingdom began to whisper. "A dragon," they said. "A great and terrible dragon has come to the mountains." The bakers whispered it while kneading bread. The farmers whispered it while feeding their goats. Even the castle cats whispered it to each other, in the way that cats do.

Everyone was afraid.

Everyone except Princess Savannah.

"I'm going to find out what's happening," she said.

"Are you sure?" said Ollie, who had just woken up from playing dead.

"I'm sure," said Savannah. "But I'd love some company."

Ollie looked at Rascal. Rascal looked at Bramble. Bramble looked at Ollie.

"We're coming with you," they said together.

Before they set off, Savannah went to visit Big Al and Wilma.

Big Al and Wilma lived in a cottage at the forest's edge, where the trees grew tall and the fire was always warm. Big Al was the biggest man Savannah had ever known — broad as a barn door, strong as a mountain, with a voice that made the floorboards hum. He knew about dragons. He'd seen one, once, a long time ago.

Wilma was elegant and beautiful, with silver hair and eyes that saw everything twice — once as it was, and once as it could be. She knew things that weren't in any book.

"A dragon in the mountains," said Big Al, stroking his chin. "Well. That's something."

"Be gentle when you get there," said Wilma. She handed Savannah a small lantern that glowed with a soft golden light. "Dragons are big, but they get scared too. And if you need her to trust you — sing. Dragons love a kind voice more than anything in the world."

Savannah hugged them both — Big Al's hug lifted her off the ground, as it always did — and set off with her friends into the forest.

The path toward the mountains wound through the oldest part of the woods, where the trees leaned in close and the light came through in patches. The farther they walked, the darker it got.

The ground rumbled again. Closer now.

Ollie froze. "I don't like this," he whispered.

"You're doing great," said Savannah. "Stay close."

Rascal found a shortcut through a shallow stream, hopping from rock to rock and waving for the others to follow. Bramble flew ahead, scouting the path, calling back: "This way! The smoke is coming from just over the ridge!"

They climbed the last hill together, side by side, and looked down into a small valley between the rocks.

And there she was.

The dragon.

She was enormous — black-scaled and shining, with wings folded tight against her body and a long tail curled around her legs. Smoke drifted from her nostrils. The ground trembled every time she shifted.

Ollie fainted. (For real this time.)

Rascal's eyes went wide. Bramble tucked herself behind Savannah's shoulder.

But Savannah looked closer.

The dragon wasn't roaring. She wasn't breathing fire. She wasn't doing anything scary at all.

She was crying.

Big, shimmering tears rolled down her dark scales and splashed onto the rocks below. Her stomach rumbled. And every few seconds, she sneezed, and a little puff of smoke came out. Not fire. Just sneezes. She had a cold.

She wasn't a monster.

She was just a dragon — alone, and hungry, and scared, and very, very far from home.

Savannah took a deep breath. She picked up the golden lantern. And she walked down into the valley.

"Hello," she said softly. "My name is Savannah."

The dragon's great head turned. Her eyes were dark and wet and enormous. She looked at Savannah the way a lost puppy looks at someone who might be kind.

"Are you okay?" said Savannah.

The dragon sniffed. She shook her head.

Savannah sat down on a rock, right there in front of the dragon, and set the lantern between them so its golden light fell gently on them both.

"You don't have to be scared," she said. "We came to help."

Rascal crept forward with an armful of blackberries he'd gathered along the way. He set them down carefully in front of the dragon. She sniffed them. Then she ate them — all of them, in one gulp — and looked at Rascal hopefully.

"I'll get more," he said, and scrambled back toward the stream.

Bramble flew to the softest patch of moss on the hillside and carried it back in her little claws, piece by piece, and piled it next to the dragon so she'd have something warm to rest on.

Ollie woke up, saw the dragon, fainted again, woke up again, and then — very bravely — tiptoed forward and patted the dragon once on the tip of her tail. "There, there," he said, his voice shaking only a little.

And then Savannah remembered what Wilma had told her.

She began to sing.

It wasn't a fancy song. It wasn't loud or complicated. It was the kind of song you hum when you're falling asleep — warm and slow and steady, like a heartbeat, like a rocking chair, like rain on a roof when you're safe inside.

The dragon closed her eyes.

Her breathing slowed.

Her tears stopped.

And when Savannah finished the song, the dragon opened her eyes and looked at her — really looked at her — and made a sound deep in her throat. Not a growl. Not a roar.

A purr.

"I think she likes you," whispered Bramble.

"I think she likes all of us," said Savannah. She reached out and placed her hand gently on the dragon's nose. The scales were warm and smooth, like stones that had been sitting in the sun.

"We should give her a name," said Rascal, returning with more berries.

Savannah looked at the dragon — dark and beautiful, with eyes like the night sky.

"Luna," she said.

And Luna purred again.

They walked home together as the sun began to set — Princess Savannah, Ollie, Rascal, Bramble, and Luna the Dragon. The forest that had seemed so dark on the way in was bright on the way back. Luna's gentle breath made the lantern glow twice as warm, and her wings sheltered them from the evening breeze.

When they reached the cottage at the forest's edge, Big Al stepped outside and looked up at the dragon and let out a long, low whistle.

"Well, I'll be," he said.

Wilma smiled. She'd known, of course. She always did.

"Welcome home, Luna," she said.

And from that day on, the kingdom wasn't afraid anymore. Because the dragon in the mountains wasn't a monster at all — she was just someone who needed a friend. And Princess Savannah, who was brave and kind, had been exactly the right person to find her.

Luna slept in the meadow beside the castle, curled up like a great dark cat, and every evening Savannah would sit with her and sing the song that Wilma taught her, and Luna would purr, and the stars would come out one by one, and everything was exactly as it should be.

And they all lived happily ever after.
