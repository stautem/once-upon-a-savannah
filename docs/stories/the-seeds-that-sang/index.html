The Seeds That Sang

~8 minutes · Princess Savannah, Luna the Dragon, Digby the Mole, Cora the Crow, Wilma

Listen to this story:

Once upon a time, on a bright spring morning when the world smelled like warm dirt and new things growing, Princess Savannah went to visit Wilma in her garden.

Wilma was kneeling beside a wooden table covered in clay pots and trowels and bundles of dried herbs. Her silver hair was swept up with a ribbon, and her hands were gentle and sure, the way they always were — whether she was planting seeds, pouring tea, or brushing the tangles from Savannah's hair.

"I have something for you," said Wilma.

She held out a small wooden box. Inside, nestled on a bed of moss, lay seven seeds. They were round and smooth, each one a different color — red, orange, yellow, green, blue, violet, and one that shimmered like gold.

And they were humming.

Each seed hummed a different note. Soft and low, like a music box hidden in a drawer. Savannah held the box close to her ear and listened.

"They're singing," she whispered.

"They are," said Wilma. "These are Song Seeds. Plant them in the garden, and they will grow into flowers that hum all evening long. A singing garden, just for you."

Savannah's eyes went wide. "Can I plant them now?"

Wilma smiled her slow, knowing smile. "I thought you might say that."

Luna was already there, of course. She had been lying in a patch of sun near the garden wall, her black scales warm and gleaming. At the word plant, her head shot up. If Savannah was digging, Luna was digging. That was the rule, and Luna had made it herself.

So Savannah dug seven small holes in a neat row, and she planted each seed carefully — red, orange, yellow, green, blue, violet, and gold. She patted the earth down gently over each one. Luna helped by digging one of the holes twice as deep as it needed to be and then looking very proud of herself.

"Water them," said Wilma, "and wait."

Savannah watered them. And she waited.

She did not have to wait long.

By the time the sun began to set, seven little stems had pushed up through the earth. Seven little buds had formed. And seven little flowers had opened their petals — each one the color of the seed it came from, each one humming its own note into the evening air.

Savannah clapped her hands. "Listen!"

She listened.

And then her smile faded. Just a little.

Because the notes did not sound right together.

The red flower hummed high. The blue flower hummed low. The gold flower hummed somewhere in between. But they were all humming at once, all going their own way, and the sound was not a song. It was a jumble. A tangle. Like seven people all trying to sing different tunes at the same time.

The flowers felt it too. Their petals drooped. Their stems bent. The humming grew quieter, sadder, as though the flowers knew they were supposed to make something beautiful and couldn't quite figure out how.

"Oh no," said Savannah softly. "They're wilting."

Luna sniffed the drooping gold flower. It let out a weak, wobbly note. Luna sneezed and looked offended.

Savannah turned to Wilma, but Wilma had already gone inside. Through the cottage window, Savannah could see her settling into her chair with a cup of tea and a gentle, patient expression that said: You'll figure it out.

Savannah sat on the garden wall and thought.

"They're in the wrong order," she said. "The notes don't match because the flowers aren't in the right places. If I move them around, maybe the song will come together."

Luna tilted her head. She had opinions about this. She always had opinions.

"I need help," said Savannah.

She found Digby the Mole at the edge of the garden, just where the soft earth met the meadow grass. He was sitting quietly in the entrance to one of his tunnels, his velvety paws folded, his small dark eyes blinking in the fading light.

"Digby," said Savannah, "can you listen to the flowers from underground? Maybe you can feel which roots want to be near each other."

Digby nodded slowly. "I can do that," he said, in his soft, deep voice. "The earth hums too, you know. You just have to be quiet enough to hear it."

She found Cora the Crow on the highest branch of the oak tree, preening her glossy black feathers and looking very important.

"Cora," called Savannah, "can you listen from up high? Maybe the song sounds different from the sky."

Cora puffed up with delight. "You need an aerial report? A bird's-ear view? A sky-level sound assessment?" She spread her wings dramatically. "I am exactly the crow for the job."

And so they began.

The first try.

Digby burrowed down beneath the garden and pressed his ear to the roots. "The red and the orange want to be close," he said, his voice muffled by the earth. "I can feel them reaching for each other."

Cora circled overhead. "From up here, the blue one sounds lonely on the end! Move it toward the middle!"

Luna dug up the blue flower — carefully, but with great enthusiasm and a shower of dirt — and Savannah replanted it between the green and the violet.

They stepped back and listened.

The flowers hummed.

Better. The red and the orange blended warmly together. The blue sounded less lonely. But the gold flower on the end clashed with the violet, and the whole thing still wobbled and wandered.

"Closer," said Savannah. "But not yet."

The flowers drooped a little less. But they were still waiting for their song.

The second try.

Digby went underground again. "The gold one," he said. "Its roots go deeper than the others. It wants to be in the center. I can feel it pulling."

Cora swooped low. "He's right! From up here, it's like the gold one is the heartbeat and everything else should go around it!"

Luna dug up the gold flower. She was even more enthusiastic this time. There was dirt on her nose, dirt on her wings, and dirt in places dirt should not be. She set the gold flower in the center of the row and sat back, tail thumping.

Savannah replanted the others around it — red, orange, gold, green, blue, violet — and they all listened.

The flowers hummed.

Much better. The gold flower in the center held the melody together like a warm hand holding a thread. The colors on either side rose and fell around it, and for a moment — just a moment — it almost sounded like a song.

But then it slipped. A gap in the melody, a place where the notes reached for something that wasn't there. The music stumbled, like a sentence missing a word.

The flowers drooped again. Not much. But enough.

"So close," said Savannah. "There's something missing."

Luna growled at the dirt, as though she could scold the answer out of the ground.

The third try.

Digby pressed his whole body flat against the earth and listened for a long, long time.

"There's something else down here," he said at last. "Another root. Deeper than the rest. But it's not humming."

"Not humming?" said Savannah.

"Silent," said Digby. "But it's there. Right in the middle of everything, underneath the gold flower."

Savannah knelt down and dug gently with her hands. Luna helped — carefully this time, soft as a dragon can be, which is not very soft but soft enough. And there, buried deeper than all the others, they found it.

An eighth seed. White as moonlight. Completely silent.

Savannah held it up. It made no sound at all. No hum, no note, no whisper. It just sat in her palm, cool and smooth and quiet.

"Why would a singing garden need a quiet seed?" asked Cora, tilting her head.

The cottage door opened, and Wilma stepped out into the garden. The last light of the day caught her silver hair, and she looked at the small white seed in Savannah's hand with a smile.

"Because," said Wilma, "every song needs a rest."

She knelt beside Savannah in the dirt.

"A rest is the pause between the notes," she said. "The quiet space that lets you hear the music. Without it, the notes have nowhere to breathe. They crowd together and stumble over each other." She touched the white seed gently. "This one doesn't sing. But it makes all the others sound right."

Savannah looked at the little white seed. Then she looked at the row of drooping, humming flowers, still trying so hard to find their song.

"Where does it go?" she asked.

"Right where you found it," said Wilma. "In the center. Between the ones that need a breath."

So Savannah planted the white seed between the gold flower and the green. She patted the earth down and watered it, and they all waited.

The white seed sprouted fast — a slender stem, pale leaves, and a single white flower that opened its petals without a sound. No hum. No note. Just a gentle, quiet bloom, glowing softly in the dusk.

And then the other flowers lifted their heads.

The red flower hummed. The orange flower answered. The yellow flower joined in, and the melody passed to the gold — warm and steady in the center — and then it reached the white flower, and there it paused.

Just for a breath.

Just long enough.

And then the green flower picked it up, and the blue, and the violet, and the song came all the way around again, and this time — this time — it was perfect.

A melody. A real one. Soft and sweet and clear, rising and falling like a lullaby, with just enough quiet in the middle to make every note shine.

Savannah sat very still and listened.

Digby closed his eyes and swayed.

Cora, for once in her life, said nothing at all.

Luna lay down right next to the flowers, pressed her chin to the ground, and let out the longest, most contented sigh a dragon has ever sighed.

Wilma put her arm around Savannah's shoulders.

"You found it," she said. "The song was always there. It just needed someone patient enough to listen for what was missing."

The singing garden bloomed every evening after that.

As the sun went down and the sky turned gold and pink and soft, the flowers would open their petals and begin to hum. The melody would rise from the garden — red, orange, yellow, gold, pause, green, blue, violet — and drift across the meadow like a lullaby carried on the breeze.

Digby could hear it underground, and it made his tunnels feel warm.

Cora could hear it from the treetops, and she told everyone in the forest about it — three times, with increasing drama.

Luna slept beside the garden every evening, curled in a great black circle around the flowers like a scaly, snoring fence. She had decided the garden was hers to protect, and that was not up for discussion.

And Savannah would sit on the garden wall with Wilma as the last light faded, listening to the flowers sing their little song — the one that only worked because of the quiet note in the middle. The one that proved that sometimes, the most important part of a melody is the place where it takes a breath.

The white flower never made a sound. But every evening, it glowed just a little brighter than the rest.

And they all lived happily ever after.
