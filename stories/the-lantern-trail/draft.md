# The Lantern Trail

**Date:** 2026-03-30
**Characters:** Princess Savannah, Luna, Bramble the Bat, Thistle the Rabbit, The Fireflies
**Reading time:** ~8 minutes

---

Once upon a time, in a green and gentle kingdom where the trees grew tall and the rivers sang, there lived a princess named Savannah.

Princess Savannah had a best friend, and her best friend was a dragon.

The dragon's name was Luna. She was big and black-scaled and beautiful, with wings like velvet and a tail that swished through the tall grass wherever she went. She was brave and smart and cuddly and very, very loud — and she went everywhere Savannah went. That was not a suggestion. That was the rule.

Every evening, when the sky turned gold and the air turned soft, Savannah and Luna walked along the forest edge. It was their favorite time. The world was quiet, the light was warm, and the trees whispered gently to each other the way old friends do.

They were never alone on their walks. Bramble the Bat came too, fluttering silently overhead, her bright little ears turning this way and that, listening to everything the forest had to say. And Thistle the Rabbit came bouncing along beside them, because Thistle came bouncing along beside everything.

"Did you hear that?" said Thistle, stopping short. "Was that a squirrel? Was that the wind? Was that my own feet? I think it was my own feet."

"It was your own feet," said Bramble, from above.

"That's what I thought," said Thistle, and kept bouncing.

It was a very good evening. Just like every evening.

But tonight, something was different.

---

They had come to the place where the meadow met the trees — the place they always turned around — when Savannah stopped.

"Look," she said.

There, between two old oaks, a path opened into the forest. A path that had not been there before.

And along the path, floating in the air like golden bubbles, were lanterns.

Small lanterns. Warm lanterns. They hung at the height of Savannah's eyes, glowing softly, golden as honey, each one no bigger than her fist. They drifted in a long, gentle line, curving away into the trees and out of sight.

Luna tilted her head. Her black scales caught the golden light.

"I've walked this edge a hundred times," said Savannah. "There's never been a path here."

"There's never been lanterns here, either," said Thistle, his nose twitching. "I would have noticed lanterns. I notice everything. Well, most things. Well, some things."

Bramble dropped lower and listened. Her ears turned and turned.

"The forest is humming," she said quietly. "Something is happening in there tonight."

Savannah looked at the lanterns. They glowed so gently. So warmly. Like someone had lit them just for her.

"Let's follow them," she said.

Luna was already walking.

---

The path was soft with moss and pine needles, and it curved through the trees like a winding river. The lanterns floated ahead, golden and patient, each one waiting for them.

And here was the strange thing.

As Savannah walked toward a lantern, it glowed brighter. Warmer. As if it were happy to see her. But when she passed it and looked back, the lantern behind her dimmed — gently, slowly, like a candle being turned down low.

The light was always ahead. Always guiding them forward.

"They want us to follow," said Savannah.

"Follow where?" said Thistle. "Into the deep dark woods? In the evening? When it's getting dark? When there are mysterious floating lanterns that nobody can explain?"

He paused.

"Because I'm in," he said. "I just wanted to say all that first."

Bramble flew ahead to scout, clicking softly. The little sounds bounced off the trees and came back to her, painting a picture of the path in her mind.

"It's clear ahead," she called back. "The path keeps going. More lanterns. Lots more."

They walked deeper.

The trees grew taller. The sky above became a narrow ribbon of purple and dark blue, threaded between the branches. The lanterns were the brightest things in the world now, glowing like drops of sunlight caught in jars.

Thistle bounded through a cluster of them and laughed as golden sparks drifted off like tiny fireworks. They settled in his fur and twinkled there, making him look like a rabbit made of stars.

"Look at me!" he cried. "I'm sparkling! I've never sparkled before!"

"You've always been a little sparkly," said Bramble, and Thistle beamed.

Luna paused beside a lantern that hung low over the path. She leaned close and breathed on it — just a soft, warm breath, the kind she used when she was being gentle.

The lantern hummed.

A single, clear note. Sweet and round, like a bell made of honey.

Luna's eyes went wide. She hurried to the next lantern and breathed on it.

A different note. Higher. Brighter.

She breathed on the next.

Lower. Deeper. Warm as a hug.

Luna looked at Savannah. Then she turned and walked down the path, breathing softly on every lantern she passed. And each one sang its note — and the notes began to layer, one on top of the other, building into a melody that floated through the trees like a lullaby the forest had always known but never sung out loud.

"Luna," Savannah whispered. "You're making them sing."

Luna held her head very high. She had opinions about music, and this music met her standards.

Savannah walked beside her dragon and listened to the melody grow. And as she listened, she looked closely at the lanterns. Really closely.

Inside each golden glow, something flickered.

Not flame. Not magic.

Wings.

Tiny, delicate wings, beating fast and bright.

"Bramble," said Savannah. "Come look at this."

Bramble swooped down and hovered beside a lantern, her ears twitching.

"They're alive," said Bramble. "I can hear their wings."

Savannah cupped her hands around the nearest lantern, very gently, the way you hold something precious. Through the warm glow, she could see them — tiny creatures with soft wings and bodies that shone like embers. Three of them, maybe four, flying in a tight little cluster, their light blending together into one golden glow.

"They're not lanterns," said Savannah. "They're fireflies."

---

The path turned one more time, around a great old tree with roots that curled like sleeping dragons, and then it opened up.

And there was the clearing.

It was wide and round and carpeted with soft clover, and it was full of light.

Hundreds of fireflies. Maybe thousands. They drifted and swirled and blinked in every shade of gold — pale yellow, deep amber, warm honey, bright as a sunrise. They filled the clearing from the clover to the treetops, turning the night into something that glowed.

It was the most beautiful thing Savannah had ever seen.

"The Firefly Gathering," said Bramble, very softly. "I've heard of this. Once a year, they all come together. Once a year, in a clearing deep in the woods."

"But why did they light the path?" asked Savannah.

The fireflies answered.

They drifted toward her — a dozen of them, then two dozen, then more — and they swirled around her in a slow, warm spiral. They blinked in a pattern. A rhythm. Like a heartbeat. Like a hello.

And Savannah understood.

"You've been watching me," she said. "On my evening walks. At the forest edge."

The fireflies blinked brighter.

"You lit the trail for me. You wanted me to come."

Brighter still. The whole clearing pulsed with warm light, and it felt like a smile.

There was nothing to fix. Nothing to solve. Nothing to save. The fireflies had simply seen a princess who walked by their forest every evening, and they had decided she belonged at their gathering.

It was an invitation. And Savannah accepted it.

"Thank you," she said.

---

They stayed for a long, lovely time.

Thistle raced through the clearing, and the fireflies chased him, and he chased them back, and they all got confused about who was chasing who, and everyone was happy about it.

Bramble hung upside-down from a low branch and watched the patterns the fireflies made, her bright eyes reflecting a thousand tiny lights.

Savannah sat in the soft clover in the middle of it all and watched the glow.

And Luna — Luna lay down beside her, stretching out her great black wings, and the fireflies came to her.

One at a time, then ten at a time, then more than anyone could count.

They landed on her wings. They settled along her back. They dotted her tail and lined her neck and nestled in the curves of her ears. They covered her black scales in points of golden light until Luna looked like a piece of the night sky — a dragon-shaped constellation, glowing and glittering and absolutely magnificent.

Luna did not move. She held very, very still — which, for Luna, was remarkable. She knew something wonderful was happening, and for once in her life, she had no opinions about it. She just let it be.

"You look beautiful," Savannah told her.

Luna blinked. One slow, happy blink. The fireflies on her eyelids blinked too.

The clearing hummed with the melody Luna had started on the trail, and the fireflies carried it now, each one singing its tiny note, filling the forest with a lullaby made of light and sound and warmth.

---

When it was very late — later than a princess usually stayed up, later than even Thistle could keep bouncing — the fireflies began to drift.

They rose from Luna's scales, one by one, leaving her dark and warm again. They lifted from the clover and the branches. And then, gently, they arranged themselves along the path — the same path, the same golden line — lighting the way home.

Savannah stood and stretched. Luna yawned — a great, wide, dragon yawn that made three fireflies wobble in the air.

"Thank you," Savannah said to the clearing. "For inviting me."

The clearing blinked.

They walked home along the lantern trail, the four of them — Savannah and Luna side by side, Bramble gliding above, Thistle bouncing softly now, sleepy but still going. The fireflies lit every step, warm and steady, until the path reached the forest edge and the meadow opened up under the stars.

Savannah turned back one last time.

At the tree line, one single firefly hovered. Just one. It blinked — slow and bright.

Goodnight.

Savannah smiled. "Goodnight," she said.

And they walked home through the meadow, under the stars, warm and full of wonder.

---

From that night on, things were a little different.

Every evening, Savannah and Luna and Bramble and Thistle still walked along the forest edge. The sky still turned gold. The trees still whispered. Thistle still heard his own feet and asked about it.

But now, at the tree line, one small light waited for them.

Just one. Blinking softly. Blinking hello.

And Savannah always stopped, and smiled, and blinked back.

Because some invitations last forever. And some friendships start with a single light in the dark, saying: *We see you. We're glad you're here. Come and glow with us.*

And they all lived happily ever after.
