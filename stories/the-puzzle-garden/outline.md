# Outline: The Puzzle Garden

**Premise:** Wilma's magical garden — where flowers only bloom when you solve the puzzle carved into each garden stone — has lost all its puzzles overnight, and the garden is wilting. Savannah must read the clues the flowers themselves leave behind to restore each stone and bring the garden back to life.
**Characters:** Princess Savannah, Luna, Wilma, Thistle the Rabbit, The Fireflies
**Setting:** Wilma's garden, tucked behind a stone cottage at the edge of the kingdom. The garden is ringed by a low mossy wall and filled with winding paths between raised stone beds. Each bed has a flat puzzle stone at its center, and when the puzzle is solved, the flowers around it burst into bloom. It's a place of color, fragrance, and quiet magic — or it was, until this morning.

## Beats

1. **The peaceful kingdom.** Savannah loves visiting Wilma's garden. We open on a memory of how it usually works — Savannah kneeling beside a stone, tracing the carved puzzle with her finger (maybe a riddle, maybe a shape to complete), and when she gets it right, the flowers around the stone yawn open in a wave of color. Luna romps between the flower beds, sniffing everything, sneezing at the pollen, and barking (roaring) at butterflies. Wilma walks the paths in her calm, knowing way, tending things with a touch. The garden hums. Everything is warm.

2. **The problem.** Savannah arrives one morning and the garden is wrong. The stones are smooth and blank — every puzzle has vanished, as if the carvings were never there. Without the puzzles, the flowers are drooping, curling closed, losing their color. Wilma is standing at the garden gate, calm but sad. She tells Savannah the puzzles disappeared in the night. She doesn't know why or how. The garden has never done this before. If the flowers close all the way, the magic may sleep for a very long time.

3. **The call.** Savannah looks at the wilting flowers and decides she's going to fix every single stone. Wilma smiles her quiet smile and says she believes Savannah can — but she'll need to listen carefully, because the flowers remember their puzzles even if the stones have forgotten. Luna is already bounding toward the first stone, nose to the ground. Thistle the Rabbit appears at the garden wall, ears up, talking a mile a minute about how he noticed the garden looked dim on his morning run and came straight here. He volunteers to help before anyone asks.

4. **The adventure.** Savannah goes stone by stone, and each one is its own small puzzle scene. The first stone sits in the sunflower bed — the sunflowers are drooping but still leaning toward the sun. Savannah notices they're all pointing the same direction and realizes the puzzle was about direction: she traces an arrow on the blank stone pointing east, toward the sunrise, and the sunflowers lift their heads and bloom. The second stone is in the vine bed — the vines have curled themselves into the shape of a spiral. Thistle spots it first (he's jittery and fast and notices everything at once, even if he announces it by shouting). Savannah traces the spiral on the stone and the vines burst into flower. Luna helps with one — a bed of warm red flowers that only bloom near heat. Luna breathes a gentle puff of dragon warmth onto the stone, and the flowers remember. Each puzzle is slightly different, and each one has a moment for a different character to contribute: Thistle's speed, Luna's fire, Savannah's patience and attention.

5. **The twist.** They reach the last stone, at the very center of the garden. It's the biggest stone, and the flowers around it are the most wilted. But this bed has no obvious clue — the flowers are too far gone to point or curl or give any hint. Savannah is stuck. Then night begins to fall, and the Fireflies arrive, drifting into the garden one by one. As they settle on the wilted flowers and the blank stone, their light reveals something no one could see in daylight — faint glowing traces on the stone, like a message written in light. The last puzzle was always a nighttime puzzle. It was meant to be solved in the dark, by the light of the Fireflies. The carvings on the stone are still there — they just only show up when the Fireflies are near. Savannah reads the glowing pattern: it's a star, the same star shape that Wilma wears on a chain around her neck. The puzzles didn't vanish because something went wrong. The garden does this on purpose, once in a great while — it resets itself so someone can solve it all fresh again, so the magic stays alive. Wilma knew. She was waiting for Savannah to be ready.

6. **The resolution.** Savannah traces the star onto the final stone. The center flowers bloom — the brightest, tallest, most colorful flowers in the whole garden — and the light ripples outward, refreshing every stone and every bed. The whole garden is glowing now, healthier and more vivid than before. Wilma puts her hand on Savannah's shoulder and tells her the garden chose well. Thistle is hopping in circles, unable to contain himself. Luna plops down in the middle of the biggest flower bed and rolls onto her back, completely satisfied.

7. **Happily ever after.** The Fireflies stay, drifting through the garden like floating lanterns. Wilma, Savannah, Luna, and Thistle sit together in the glow. The garden hums the way it always has — but a little louder now, a little warmer. Savannah knows she can come back anytime. The puzzles will be here, the flowers will be here, and the magic will keep growing as long as someone is willing to look closely and listen. Luna is already asleep in the flowers, snoring softly, one paw over her nose. Goodnight, garden. Goodnight, Savannah.
