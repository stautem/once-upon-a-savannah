# The Locked-Up Lullabies

**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Characters:** Princess Savannah, Luna, Rascal the Raccoon, Hazel the Owl, Big Al
**Reading time:** ~8 minutes

---

Once upon a time, on a soft summer evening, the village square was glowing.

Lanterns hung from every market stall, gold and warm, swaying just a little in the breeze. The cobblestones were still warm from the afternoon sun. The big old fountain in the center of the square splashed quietly, and the first stars were beginning to peek out above the rooftops.

Princess Savannah sat on the edge of the fountain, swinging her feet and watching the evening settle in.

Luna the dragon was sprawled across the warm cobblestones nearby, taking up far more space than any one dragon needed to. She yawned an enormous yawn — the kind of yawn that showed every single tooth and made a sound like a creaky door. Then she rolled onto her back and looked at anyone who walked by with big, hopeful eyes.

"Belly scratches," Luna rumbled. "Someone. Anyone. Please."

A passing hedgehog gave her a polite pat and kept walking.

"That barely counts," said Luna.

Up on the roof of a market stall, Rascal the Raccoon was poking through someone's wind chimes, making them jingle and clang.

"Rascal," Savannah called up, "those don't belong to you."

"I'm not taking them," said Rascal. "I'm investigating them. There's a difference."

Hazel the Owl was perched on the fountain beside Savannah, her big golden eyes turned up toward the sky. She tilted her head one way. Then the other way. Then the first way again.

"Why," said Hazel, to nobody in particular, "is the moon a different shape every single night?"

Nobody answered, because nobody ever could, and Hazel didn't seem to mind.

It was the kind of evening where nothing needed to happen, and that was perfectly fine.

But then — something happened anyway.

A gentle old man came walking into the square, pulling a little wooden cart behind him. He had a long gray coat and kind, tired eyes, and his name was Wendell. He was a puzzle-maker, and he had traveled a very long way.

On his cart sat the most beautiful music box anyone had ever seen.

It was carved with stars and moons and tiny sleeping animals, polished until it seemed to glow from the inside. Everyone in the square stopped to look. Even Rascal climbed down from the roof to get a closer peek.

"Oh," breathed Savannah. "It's lovely."

"It is," said Wendell, with a small, tired smile. "But it's locked."

He turned the box so they could see. Seven tiny locks ran along the front, each one different from the last. Seven little puzzles, all in a row.

"I built this box myself, a long time ago," said Wendell. "I put something precious inside. But the locks were enchanted, and now even I can't remember how they work. I've carried it to every village in the kingdom. Clever folk have tried. Strong folk have tried. Patient folk have tried." He shook his head. "Nobody has opened all seven."

He set the box down on the edge of the fountain. "I just want to hear what's inside one more time."

Savannah looked at the box. She looked at Wendell's tired, kind face. She looked at her friends.

Luna was already pressing her ear against the box, her black scales gleaming in the lantern light. Her eyes went wide. She nudged Savannah with her big snout — urgent, excited.

"There's something in there," Luna whispered. "I can hear it. Something soft. Something beautiful."

That settled it.

"I'd like to try," said Savannah. "If that's all right."

"Of course," said Wendell. "Everyone is welcome to try."

Savannah gathered her friends. Rascal, because he was clever with his paws and could find a solution in just about anything. Hazel, because she thought carefully and always asked the right questions.

And she went to find Big Al.

Big Al lived in the stone workshop at the edge of the square. He was big and strong and quiet, the kind of man who made you feel safe just by being nearby. When Savannah brought him the music box, he turned it over in his huge, careful hands and studied it for a long time.

"Seven locks," he said. "You don't need to be the smartest to open them. You just need to be patient enough to listen to each one."

Then he settled down on a bench, folded his arms, and watched.

---

The first lock was a sliding tile puzzle. Nine tiny tiles that needed to be rearranged into a picture.

Rascal cracked his knuckles — all of them — and went to work. His quick little paws slid the tiles this way and that, click-click-click.

"What's the picture supposed to be?" he asked.

Savannah leaned close. "A crescent moon," she said. "That piece goes there — and that one slides up —"

Click-click-click. Rascal's paws flew. The tiles slid into place, and the crescent moon appeared, and the first lock popped open with a soft *snick*.

"One down," said Rascal, dusting off his paws. "Six to go."

---

The second lock was a pattern of tiny silver stars. They needed to be pressed in the right order.

Hazel hopped closer and studied them. She tilted her head one way. Then the other. Then the first way again.

"I know these," she said softly. "These stars — they match the constellation I watch every night. The one that looks like a sleeping fox."

She pressed the stars, one by one, in just the right order. First the nose. Then the ears. Then the curled-up tail.

The second lock clicked open.

"Two down," said Savannah. "Five to go."

---

The third lock had a riddle etched into the brass in tiny, curling letters. Savannah read it aloud:

*"I have no voice, but I can make you sleep. I have no arms, but I hold you."*

Everyone went quiet and thought.

"A blanket?" said Rascal.

"A cloud?" said Hazel.

Luna rumbled, "A very warm dragon?"

Savannah smiled. She thought for a moment longer, quiet and still. Then she leaned close to the lock and whispered, "A lullaby."

*Click.*

The third lock opened.

"Three down," said Savannah. "Four to go."

---

The fourth lock had a tiny keyhole, no bigger than a berry. And the keyhole was shaped like a dragon.

Everyone looked at Luna.

Luna looked at the keyhole. She looked at her claw. She looked at the keyhole again.

"It's me-shaped," she said, delighted.

She pressed her claw into the lock, very gently. It fit perfectly, like it had been made just for her.

*Click.*

Luna pranced. She pranced in a circle. She pranced around the fountain. She pranced back.

"Did you see that?" she said. "It was me-shaped! It was a Luna lock!"

"We saw," said Savannah, laughing.

"Four down," said Rascal. "Three to go."

---

The fifth lock was a knot of silver thread, tangled tight, that needed to be untangled before the lock would release.

This one was tricky. Rascal tried his quick paws, but the thread was delicate and kept catching. Savannah tried her careful fingers, but some of the loops were too small.

So they worked together. Rascal held the thread steady while Savannah teased the knots apart, one tiny loop at a time. Patient and slow. Patient and slow.

It took a while. The lanterns swayed. The fountain splashed. Nobody rushed.

And finally, the last loop of silver thread came free, and the fifth lock opened.

"Five down," said Savannah. "Two to go."

---

The sixth lock had no keyhole and no puzzle. It just hummed — a low, steady note, like a tuning fork.

"It wants a sound," said Hazel. "It wants the right note."

Hazel hummed low. The lock didn't move.

Savannah hummed high. The lock didn't move.

Luna leaned in close and let out a long, rumbling purr. Low and warm and steady, the way she purred when she was sleepy and happy and full of love.

The lock shivered. It shimmered. And it clicked open.

"My purr is magic," said Luna, very seriously.

"Six down," said Savannah. "One to go."

---

The seventh lock was blank.

No puzzle. No riddle. No keyhole. No stars, no thread, no tiles. Just smooth, warm wood.

They all stared at it.

Rascal tried pushing it. Nothing.

Hazel tried tapping it in patterns. Nothing.

Luna tried purring at it again. Nothing.

Savannah tried turning it, sliding it, whispering to it. Nothing and nothing and nothing.

They sat back. The square was quiet. The lanterns glowed. Wendell watched from his cart, his hands folded, his eyes gentle.

Big Al leaned forward on his bench. He looked at Savannah with his calm, steady gaze, and he said, very gently:

"Some locks don't need solving, little one. They just need asking."

Savannah looked at the blank lock for a long moment. She placed her hand flat against the smooth, warm wood. And she said, simply and quietly:

"Please."

*Click.*

The seventh lock opened.

It had never been a puzzle at all. It just needed someone kind enough to ask.

Wendell's eyes shone bright. He pressed his hands together and nodded, again and again.

"That's the one," he said, his voice thick with feeling. "That's the one nobody ever tried. Everyone was so busy being clever, nobody thought to just be gentle."

---

The music box opened slowly, like a flower blooming.

And from inside rose the most beautiful lullaby anyone had ever heard.

It was soft. It was warm. It was golden, like someone humming you to sleep on a summer night. It floated up from the box and drifted out over the square, through the market stalls, past the fountain, over the rooftops, and into the whole village.

Rascal curled up right where he was and tucked his tail over his nose.

Hazel's big golden eyes drifted shut on the fountain ledge.

Luna laid her great big head in Savannah's lap and let out the longest, happiest sigh.

Even Big Al leaned back on his bench and closed his eyes, with a small, warm smile on his face.

Wendell listened with his hands clasped, finally hearing his lullaby again after all that time. A tear rolled down his cheek, but it was the happy kind.

The whole village yawned. The whole village smiled. The whole village began to sleep.

---

Wendell gave the music box to Savannah the very next morning.

"It belongs with someone who knows how to listen," he said. "And someone who remembers to say please."

And every night after that, when the stars came out and the lanterns glowed and the cobblestones were warm, Savannah would carry the music box to the square and open it, lock by lock by lock.

Rascal would slide the tiles. Hazel would press the stars. Savannah would whisper the riddle's answer. They'd untangle the silver thread together, patient and slow. Hazel and Savannah and Luna would hum until the sixth lock shivered open.

And Savannah would place her hand on the seventh lock and say, "Please."

Luna, of course, always insisted on pressing the dragon-shaped lock herself. Every single night. She would not let anyone else do it. It was her lock, and that was that.

And every night, the lullaby would rise from the box and drift over the village — soft and warm and golden — and everyone would sleep a little deeper and dream a little sweeter.

And if you listen very carefully, on a warm summer evening, you can still hear it playing. Soft and golden, all the way to the edge of the kingdom.

And all the way to you.

Goodnight.
