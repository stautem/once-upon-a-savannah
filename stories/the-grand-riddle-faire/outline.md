# Outline: The Grand Riddle Faire

**Premise:** Every year the kingdom holds a Grand Riddle Faire, and this year Savannah enters as the youngest competitor ever — only to discover the final puzzle can only be solved by two people working together.
**Characters:** Princess Savannah, Luna, Finnegan the Fox, Mossy the Turtle, Wilma
**Setting:** The kingdom's Great Green, a wide sunny meadow transformed once a year into a dazzling faire with colorful tents, wooden puzzle-stages, winding hedge-mazes, and banners fluttering from every post. At its center stands the ancient Riddle Tower — a mossy stone structure where the final puzzle has gone unsolved for a hundred years.

## Beats

1. **The peaceful kingdom.** It's the morning of the Grand Riddle Faire, and the whole kingdom is buzzing. Savannah and Luna walk through the meadow watching workers string up lanterns and stack puzzle-boxes on wooden tables. Finnegan the Fox is already there, darting between booths, trying to peek at the riddles early. Mossy the Turtle is sitting on a stump near the entrance, slowly reading the rules for the third time. Savannah loves riddles — she and Luna play guessing games every night before bed, Luna barking out wrong answers on purpose just to make Savannah laugh. This year, for the first time, Savannah has decided to enter the competition. She's the youngest competitor by far, and the crowd murmurs when she signs her name on the big chalkboard.

2. **The problem.** The Faire has three rounds, and competitors are eliminated at each stage. The real tension isn't danger — it's nerves. Savannah is excited but jittery. The other competitors are older, louder, and very confident. One in particular — a tall, serious badger named Bramford — has won the last three years running and acts like the whole Faire belongs to him. He scoffs when he sees Savannah's name on the board. Luna growls at him. Finnegan whispers that Bramford isn't mean, exactly — he's just so focused on winning that he's forgotten how to have fun. Mossy says, slowly and carefully, "Being nervous just means you care about something. That's not a bad thing."

3. **The call.** Savannah decides she's going to try her best, not because she needs to win, but because riddles are fun and she wants to see how far she can go. Luna will be her loudest cheerleader from the sidelines (literally — she roars after every round). Finnegan appoints himself her "riddle coach," offering increasingly unhelpful hints. Mossy stations himself near the puzzle-stages so Savannah can see his calm, steady face whenever she feels rushed. Wilma appears at the edge of the Faire, elegant and unhurried, and gives Savannah a small piece of advice before the first round: "The best puzzle-solvers aren't the fastest, dear. They're the ones who look at things from more than one side."

4. **The adventure.** Round one is the Maze-Boxes — wooden contraptions with sliding panels, hidden latches, and false bottoms. Savannah works through hers carefully, turning it over the way Wilma suggested, and finds the hidden marble inside. Several competitors are eliminated. Round two is the Riddle-Gates — a series of tall wooden gates, each with a riddle carved into the arch. You can only pass through if you answer correctly. Savannah solves them one by one, sometimes pausing, sometimes laughing when she gets the trick. Finnegan shouts a wrong answer from the crowd and then covers his mouth. Luna bounces up and down so hard the ground shakes. By the end of round two, only Savannah and Bramford remain. The crowd goes quiet. It's time for the final puzzle — the one in the Riddle Tower that no one has solved in a hundred years.

5. **The twist.** Savannah and Bramford climb the stone steps into the Riddle Tower. Inside, the final puzzle is carved into the floor — a huge stone circle split into two halves, each half covered in symbols. There are two sets of handles on opposite sides of the room. Savannah studies it and realizes: the symbols on her half are the answers to the symbols on Bramford's half, and his are the answers to hers. It's not a puzzle for one person. It's a puzzle for two. Bramford is already pulling at his handles alone, frustrated, trying to brute-force it. He's been trying to solve this puzzle by himself for three years. Savannah could stay on her side, work her half, and wait for him to fail. But that's not how the puzzle works — and it's not who she is.

6. **The resolution.** Savannah walks across the room and says, "I think we're supposed to do this together." Bramford hesitates. He's spent so long trying to win on his own that asking for help — or accepting it — feels impossible. But Savannah doesn't push. She just starts reading his symbols out loud, offering her answers freely. Slowly, Bramford starts reading hers back. They call answers across the circle. Luna, listening from outside the tower window, barks encouragement. The stone halves begin to turn. Click by click, the circle comes together. When the last piece locks into place, the tower fills with warm golden light, and the puzzle opens to reveal a small chest with two matching silver pins shaped like interlocking puzzle pieces. There was never meant to be one winner. The riddle's answer, all along, was partnership.

7. **Happily ever after.** Savannah and Bramford walk out of the tower together, each wearing a silver pin, and the crowd erupts. Bramford is smiling — really smiling — for the first time all day. He thanks Savannah quietly and says he'd forgotten that puzzles were supposed to be fun. Finnegan immediately claims he knew the answer the whole time. Mossy says, "Some things are worth being slow about." Wilma catches Savannah's eye from the crowd and gives her a small, knowing nod. Luna tackles Savannah in a hug so enthusiastic they both roll into the grass. The Faire goes on into the evening — lanterns glowing, music playing, friends sharing riddles just for the joy of it. And Savannah falls asleep that night with a silver pin on her nightstand and Luna curled up warm at her feet, dreaming of next year's puzzles.
