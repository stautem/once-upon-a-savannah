# Story: The Garden That Grew Backwards

**Date:** 2026-03-30
**Characters:** Princess Savannah, Luna the Dragon, Digby the Mole, Pip the Jumping Spider, Rosie the Mole Cricket (new)
**Reading time:** ~8 minutes

---

Once upon a time, at the edge of a sunny meadow, there lived a princess named Savannah who had the most wonderful garden in all the land.

Every morning, before the dew had dried, Savannah would step outside and say the same thing she always said:

"Good morning, garden. Good morning, sun. Good morning, everyone."

And every morning, the garden seemed to answer. The sunflowers turned their big golden faces toward her. The tomatoes glowed red and warm on the vine. The carrots poked their feathery green tops up through the soil as if to wave.

Luna the dragon was always there, too. She liked to sleep in the garden, stretched out in the warm dirt between the rows with her black scales soaking up the sun. She had made herself a perfect dragon-shaped dent in the earth, and she fit into it just right. If you tried to plant something in Luna's spot, she would give you a look. You would not try again.

Digby the Mole lived in a cozy burrow at the garden's edge. He was soft and round and velvety, and he spoke in a quiet voice that made you feel like everything was going to be all right. He knew every tunnel and hidden path beneath the meadow.

And Pip the Jumping Spider bounced along the tops of the leaves each morning, inspecting every dewdrop and every petal. She was no bigger than a button, but her bright little eyes noticed everything.

It was a good garden. A happy garden. The kind of garden where things grew tall and sweet and slow.

Until the morning things started growing backwards.

---

It began with the sunflowers.

Savannah came out one morning and stopped. She tilted her head. She squinted.

"Were you always that tall?" she asked the nearest sunflower.

The sunflower did not answer, of course. But Savannah was sure — absolutely sure — that yesterday it had been taller. Just a little bit. Just enough to notice.

She shook her head and said, "Good morning, garden. Good morning, sun. Good morning, everyone."

The next day, the carrots had sunk deeper into the soil. Their green tops were shorter, as though they were being pulled down, one tiny tug at a time.

The day after that, the tomatoes had changed from red to green, and the green ones had shrunk to tiny buds. The beans had curled back up into their pods. The pumpkin vine had wound itself into a tighter and tighter spiral, like it was trying to roll itself back into a seed.

The whole garden was undoing itself.

Savannah stood in the middle of it all and said, very quietly, "Good morning, garden. Good morning, sun. Good morning, everyone."

But the garden didn't seem to answer back the way it used to.

Luna lifted her big head from her dirt bed and sniffed the air. She made a low, rumbly sound — not a growl, not a purr, something in between. She could tell something was wrong, too.

---

"The roots," said Digby.

He had come up from his burrow to look, pressing one soft velvet paw against the soil. He closed his eyes and listened the way only a mole can listen — with his whole self.

"The roots are being pulled," he said. "Something down below is tugging them. Down and down and down."

Pip bounced onto Savannah's shoulder and pointed one tiny leg at a carrot top poking out of the dirt. "Look! Look, look, look!" she said. "Tiny claw marks! On the roots! Something very small has been holding on and pulling!"

Savannah knelt down and looked. She couldn't see the marks — they were too small for princess eyes. But she trusted Pip. Pip always saw what no one else could see.

"Then we need to go down there," said Savannah. "We need to find out who's pulling my garden underground."

"I know the tunnels," said Digby. "I can lead you. Every path, every turn, every stone."

"I'll spot the clues!" said Pip, bouncing with excitement. "I'll find the trail!"

Luna stood up. She shook the dirt off her scales. She looked at the tunnel entrance by Digby's burrow. It was small. She was not small. She was, in fact, a very large dragon.

She looked at Savannah. She looked at the tunnel. She looked back at Savannah.

Then she walked straight to the tunnel entrance and began to squeeze herself in.

"Luna," said Savannah. "You won't fit."

Luna kept squeezing.

"Luna, you really won't —"

Luna wedged herself in up to her shoulders and got stuck. She huffed. She wriggled. She made a very undignified noise. Then she pushed harder, and with a pop and a shower of dirt, she was through.

A muffled roar came from inside the tunnel. It meant: *I'm fine. Keep up.*

Savannah smiled. "All right, then. Everyone's coming."

---

Down they went.

Digby led the way, padding softly through the dark earth. Savannah followed, bent low, her hand trailing along the cool tunnel wall. Pip rode on Savannah's shoulder, her bright eyes glowing like tiny lanterns. And behind them came Luna — squeezing, grunting, grumbling, and absolutely refusing to turn back.

*Deeper and deeper. Darker and warmer. Closer and closer.*

The tunnels twisted and turned. Digby chose each path without hesitation — left, right, down, down, down. The air grew warm and soft, like being wrapped in a blanket fresh from the sun.

"There," whispered Pip. "Thread-thin root trails. Something dragged them this way."

*Deeper and deeper. Darker and warmer. Closer and closer.*

The tunnels grew wider. Tiny glowing mushrooms appeared along the walls, casting a pale blue-green light that made everything shimmer. An underground stream trickled past, clear as glass. Luna stopped grumbling. Even she had to admit it was beautiful down here.

Pip spotted scratches in the tunnel wall — tiny claw marks, careful and neat, like someone had marked the way home.

"We're close," said Digby, very quietly.

*Deeper and deeper. Darker and warmer. Closer and closer.*

The tunnel opened into a cavern.

And there, in the soft glow of a hundred tiny mushrooms, was a garden.

---

But it was growing upside down.

Roots hung from the ceiling of the cavern like chandeliers, and from those roots grew flowers and vegetables — dangling downward, reaching toward the cavern floor. Sunflower heads hung like golden lanterns. Tiny tomatoes clustered in bunches overhead. Carrot roots pointed down like orange icicles.

Savannah's garden. All of it. Growing backwards, underground.

And tending it — carefully, gently, humming a little song to herself — was the smallest creature Savannah had ever seen.

She was a mole cricket. Brown and round and no bigger than Savannah's thumb. She had soft digging claws and a sweet, serious face, and she was standing on a tiny mound of earth, reaching up to adjust a dangling root with the greatest care in the world.

She didn't notice them at first.

Then Luna sneezed.

It wasn't a small sneeze. The mushroom dust had been tickling her nose for three tunnels, and a dragon can only hold a sneeze for so long. The sneeze shook the cavern. Glowing mushrooms wobbled. The underground stream splashed.

The little mole cricket spun around, her eyes wide.

"Oh!" she said. "Oh, oh no. Oh dear."

She looked at Savannah. She looked at the upside-down garden. She looked back at Savannah. And her small face crumpled.

"I'm sorry," she whispered. "I'm so, so sorry. I didn't mean to — well, I did mean to, but I didn't mean for you to — oh, this is terrible."

"Hey," said Savannah, kneeling down so they were almost eye to eye. "It's all right. I'm Savannah. What's your name?"

"Rosie," said the mole cricket, in a very small voice.

"Rosie," said Savannah. "Can you tell me about your garden?"

---

Rosie told them everything.

She lived underground, alone. She had always lived underground. And she loved it — the warm earth, the quiet, the glowing mushrooms. But she had never been able to grow anything.

Then one day, she tunneled close to the surface and heard a voice, warm and kind, saying:

*"Good morning, garden. Good morning, sun. Good morning, everyone."*

Rosie couldn't see the sun. She couldn't see the garden. But she pressed her face against the underside of the soil and she could feel it — all those roots reaching down, alive and warm, brushing against the earth like fingers.

She wanted a garden of her own, more than she had ever wanted anything.

So she began to pull. Gently. Carefully. One root at a time. She didn't know how to make things grow up, but she knew how to bring them down. She replanted each root in her cavern ceiling, and she tended them, and she hummed to them, and slowly, slowly, they began to grow for her — backwards and upside down, but growing all the same.

"I know it was wrong," said Rosie. She looked down at her small digging claws. "I just wanted a garden to say good morning to."

The cavern was quiet.

Then Savannah sat down on the soft earth, right there in the glow of the mushrooms, and said, "Show me the rest."

Rosie blinked. "You're not angry?"

"Show me," said Savannah.

So Rosie showed her. The crystal-clear stream that watered the roots from below. The clusters of glowing mushrooms arranged like little lamps. The soft, warm earth packed and smoothed into garden beds on the cavern ceiling. It was beautiful.

"Rosie," said Savannah. "What if we shared?"

"Shared?"

"What if we built a garden that grows both ways? Roots that go deep for you and stems that grow tall for me. A garden that lives above and below at the same time. Your garden and my garden — the same garden."

Rosie's eyes went wide. "Can you do that?"

Savannah grinned. "Let's find out."

---

They got to work.

Digby dug connecting tunnels between Rosie's cavern and the garden above — gentle sloping paths so Rosie could visit the surface and Savannah could visit below. The tunnels were smooth and round and perfect, because Digby always knew exactly where to dig.

Pip scouted every inch of both gardens, above and below, and found the best spots for new seeds. "Here!" she would bounce. "And here! And here, here, here!" She was very thorough.

Luna helped in her own Luna way. She lay down in the connecting tunnel and rolled back and forth until the earth was packed flat and smooth. Then she crawled into Rosie's cavern and did the same, pressing it firm and even with her big warm belly. Rosie watched this enormous black dragon rolling around in her home and didn't know quite what to say.

"That's Luna," said Savannah. "She helps like that."

Luna gave a satisfied rumble and a cloud of mushroom dust puffed up around her.

And Rosie planted from below while Savannah planted from above. They chose flowers with deep roots and tall stems, and vegetables that grew long and strong in both directions. They planted seeds and watered them from the stream below and the watering can above.

When they were done, Savannah stood in her garden in the late afternoon sunshine. The soil beneath her feet was full of new seeds and new friends and new tunnels that connected her world to Rosie's world, all the way down.

---

The new garden grew twice as well as the old one.

The sunflowers stood taller than ever, their roots reaching all the way down to Rosie's glowing cavern. The tomatoes grew red and round, loved from both sides. The carrots grew so long that Rosie could polish them like pillars in her underground home.

Every morning, Savannah stepped outside into the sunshine, and Luna stretched out in her dragon-shaped dent in the warm dirt, and Digby popped up from his burrow, and Pip bounced along the dewy leaves.

And Savannah would say, the way she always did:

"Good morning, garden. Good morning, sun. Good morning, everyone."

And from below the soil, in a soft, happy voice, Rosie would answer:

"Good morning, Savannah."

And the garden grew — up and down, above and below, together.

And they all lived happily ever after.
