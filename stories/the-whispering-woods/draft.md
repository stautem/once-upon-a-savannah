# Story: The Whispering Woods

**Date:** 2026-03-30
**Characters:** Princess Savannah, Luna, Bramble the Bat, Clover the Deer, Ollie the Opossum
**Reading time:** ~8 minutes

---

Once upon a time, on the very last day of winter, Princess Savannah and her dragon Luna went walking along the edge of the Great Forest.

It was a cold, quiet morning. The kind of morning where the whole world seems to hold its breath.

The trees were bare. The ground was dusted with frost that sparkled like sugar in the pale sunlight, and everything was waiting — though for what, no one could quite say.

Savannah wore a long woolly scarf wrapped twice around her neck. Luna wore nothing at all, because dragons do not believe in scarves, but she was huffing little clouds of warm breath that hung in the air like smoke rings.

Luna stomped in a frozen puddle. It cracked under her foot with a satisfying crunch.

She stomped in another one.

*Crunch.*

And another.

*Crunch, crunch, crunch.*

"Luna," said Savannah, smiling, "you are going to run out of puddles."

Luna looked at her as if that was the most ridiculous thing she had ever heard, and stomped in one more for good measure.

They were just about to turn for home when something came tumbling out of the forest.

It was Ollie the Opossum.

He was wide-eyed, puff-tailed, and running so fast his little legs were a gray blur. He skidded to a stop right in front of Savannah, panting hard.

"Princess! Princess! The trees — the deep woods — the whispering — I was just — and then they started — you won't believe —"

And then, right in the middle of his sentence, Ollie fainted.

He flopped onto his back, legs in the air, tongue hanging out, perfectly still.

Luna sniffed him.

He popped one eye open. "I'm fine. I do that. Where was I?"

"The whispering," said Savannah.

"Right!" Ollie scrambled to his feet. "The trees are whispering! Deep in the old part of the forest — the part where no one goes. Whispering and whispering, all day and all night!"

A soft flutter of wings, and Bramble the Bat landed on a low branch beside them, folding her dark wings neatly.

"He's right," said Bramble quietly. "I fly through the deep woods every night. The whispering started three days ago. I don't know what it is. But it's real."

A rustle at the tree line. Clover the Deer stepped out from the shadows — tall, graceful, silent. She looked at Savannah. She looked at the forest. She nodded once.

The forest was talking.

---

Savannah stood very still and listened.

At first she heard only the normal sounds — wind in the bare branches, a distant bird, Luna crunching one last puddle. But underneath all of that, she heard it.

A whisper. Not words, exactly. More like the feeling of words. Like someone speaking very far away, or very softly, or both.

Ollie shivered. "See? Spooky."

But Savannah tilted her head.

She remembered something Wilma had told her once, on a night when the wind was howling and everything outside sounded big and wild. Wilma had said, very gently, "When something sounds frightening, listen closer. Fear makes things louder than they are."

So Savannah cupped her hands behind her ears and listened closer.

The whisper didn't sound angry.

It didn't sound scary.

It sounded... frustrated. Like someone trying very, very hard to say something, and not being heard. Like someone calling from the bottom of a well.

"That's not spooky," said Savannah. "That's someone who needs help."

She looked at her friends. "I'm going to find out what the forest is trying to say. Who's coming with me?"

Luna stepped forward immediately. She did not need to be asked twice.

Bramble spread her wings. "I know every path in the dark. I'll scout ahead."

Clover dipped her head and moved to Savannah's side, quiet and steady.

Ollie looked at the forest. He looked at Savannah. He swallowed hard.

"I'll protect the rear," he said bravely. Then he scurried up Luna's tail, across her back, and settled between her shoulder blades. "From up here. Where it's safe. I mean strategic."

And in they went.

---

The forest changed as they walked deeper.

At the edges, the trees were young and thin, with bare twigs that clicked together in the breeze. But step by step, the trees grew older. Taller. Thicker. Their trunks were dark and rough, covered in moss that was soft and deep and green. Their branches wove together high overhead, like the arched ceiling of a great hall.

Bramble flew ahead, her dark wings silent against the green light. "This way," she called softly. "Stay on the path."

Clover walked beside Savannah, her hooves quiet on the mossy ground. She didn't speak, but her presence was warm and steady, and somehow that was enough.

Luna huffed warm dragon-breath as they walked, and it glowed faintly orange in the dim light. Ollie, still perched on her back, wrapped his tail around one of her spines and held on tight.

"Is it getting louder?" whispered Ollie.

It was. The whispering filled the air now — not from any one direction, but from everywhere. From the bark of the trees, from the moss underfoot, from the still air itself.

"I don't like this," said Ollie.

"Listen," said Savannah.

Ollie listened. The whisper was warm. Not sharp or hissing or cold. Round and soft, like the sound of someone humming a song they almost remember but can't quite find the words for.

"Oh," said Ollie. "That's... actually not so bad."

They walked on. The trees got older still, so wide it would take ten Savannahs holding hands to reach around them. The light that filtered through the branches was green and gold and gentle, like light through stained glass.

And the whispering grew clearer. Not louder — clearer. As if the voice was pushing toward the surface like a green shoot pushing through soil.

"We're close," said Bramble, landing on Savannah's shoulder. "Just ahead. There's a clearing."

---

They stepped into the clearing, and everyone stopped.

The Great Grandmother Oak stood in the center.

She was the biggest tree anyone had ever seen. Her trunk was wider than a house, and it rose up and up and up until her branches spread across the sky like arms reaching out to hold the whole forest. Her bark was thick and deep, carved with whorls and ridges that looked, if you tilted your head just right, like stories.

Her roots spread out in every direction, running across the clearing and diving into the earth, reaching toward the rest of the forest like fingers laced through the soil.

And she was whispering.

The sound came from deep inside her trunk. It rose through her bark, out along her branches, and into the air. Not wind. Not creaking wood. A voice — ancient and enormous and trying so very hard to speak.

But what came out were only whispers.

"She's beautiful," breathed Savannah.

Luna sat down and stared up at the Great Grandmother Oak with her head tilted to one side. Even she was quiet.

Bramble hung from a low branch and said softly, "She's been here longer than anything. Longer than the kingdom. Longer than the stories about the kingdom."

Clover stepped forward and pressed her nose gently against the bark.

Savannah walked forward and placed her hand flat against the great trunk. It was warm. Warmer than the air, warmer than the ground. Alive.

She listened. And this time, this close, she could almost hear the words.

*...coming... I can feel them... in my roots... the first ones are ready... please... can anyone...*

"She's not trying to scare anyone," said Savannah. "She's trying to tell us something. Spring is coming — she can feel it in her roots. But she's been speaking for so long without anyone listening that she's almost forgotten how."

The whispering faltered, as if the old tree had heard her and was surprised.

Savannah didn't try to fix anything. She didn't look for a spell or a potion or a magical key. She didn't try to make the tree louder or teach her new words.

She sat down right there among the Great Grandmother Oak's roots, on a cushion of soft moss. She leaned her back against the enormous trunk and felt its warmth press against her shoulders.

"I'm listening," she said.

And she waited.

Luna padded over and curled up beside her, resting her big black head on Savannah's lap. Bramble settled on a root nearby, wrapping her wings around herself like a small dark blanket. Clover folded her legs gracefully and lay down in the moss. Even Ollie crept over and sat still — really, truly still — for perhaps the first time in his life.

The whispering continued — soft and uncertain, stopping and starting, like someone clearing their throat before a speech they have practiced a thousand times but never given.

Savannah didn't fidget. She didn't hurry. She just listened the way Wilma had taught her — with her whole self, patient and open and kind.

A minute passed. The whisper grew steadier.

Another minute. The words began to form.

*"Spring..."*

Savannah smiled. "I hear you."

*"Spring... is... here."*

And the voice was not a whisper anymore. It was soft, yes. Gentle. But it was a voice — warm and deep and full, like the sound of wind through a forest of leaves, like the hum of the earth itself.

*"Spring is here,"* said the Great Grandmother Oak. *"I have been waiting so long to tell someone."*

A green shoot pushed up through the moss at Savannah's feet.

Then another. And another.

Tiny buds appeared on the Great Grandmother Oak's branches — hundreds of them, pale green and trembling. They swelled and opened into small, perfect leaves that caught the light and held it.

Flowers bloomed across the clearing floor — white ones first, then yellow, then pale purple, pushing up through the moss like stars coming out at evening. They spread outward from the great tree, racing along the roots, flowing out of the clearing and into the forest beyond.

Luna lifted her head and watched a flower bloom right between her front paws. She sniffed it. Her tail thumped against the ground.

Ollie stared as a cluster of tiny white blossoms sprouted from the moss beside him. "Oh," he said very quietly. "Oh, that's lovely."

Bramble unfolded one wing and watched a vine of pale green leaves curl gently around the root where she was resting. She smiled her small bat smile.

Clover stood, and where she had been lying, a patch of soft clover — her namesake — had sprung up, thick and green.

The Great Grandmother Oak rustled all her new leaves at once, and the sound was not a whisper anymore. It was full and warm and glad.

*"Thank you,"* she said. *"I just needed someone to listen."*

---

By the time Savannah and her friends walked out of the forest, the whole world had changed.

The bare branches were covered in buds. The frost had melted into tiny streams that trickled between the roots. The air was warmer — the soft, promising warmth of early spring, the kind that says the good part is just beginning.

A gentle breeze followed them out of the woods, carrying the smell of new flowers and damp earth waking up.

Ollie walked on his own two feet the whole way back, tail held high. "I was brave," he announced. "I sat still for five whole minutes."

"A new record," agreed Bramble.

Luna stomped in a puddle that was no longer frozen. It splashed. She looked deeply satisfied.

At the edge of the forest, Savannah turned and looked back. The trees that had been bare and gray that morning were soft with green now, and if she listened — really listened — she could hear the Great Grandmother Oak, far away in her clearing, speaking in her full, warm voice. Telling the forest her news. Telling the roots and the branches and the sleeping seeds in the soil.

Spring is here. Spring is here. Wake up and grow.

Savannah smiled.

"I'll come back," she said. "Every year, when winter ends, I'll come and listen."

The trees rustled — not a whisper, but a warm, full, happy sound.

Like laughter. Like a thank-you. Like the forest saying, *We'll be here.*

And from that day on, the Great Forest bloomed earlier than any other forest in the land, because at its heart there lived an ancient, wonderful tree who had found her voice again — all because a princess sat down, leaned back, and listened.

And they all lived happily ever after.
