# The Lantern Festival

**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Characters:** Princess Savannah, Luna, Finnegan the Fox, Hazel the Owl, Wilma
**Reading time:** ~8 minutes

---

Once upon a time, in a kingdom beside a wide, still lake, the first golden day of autumn arrived.

The leaves on the old oak trees turned orange and red and butter-yellow. The air smelled like cinnamon and woodsmoke. And the whole kingdom was buzzing with excitement — because tonight was the Lantern Festival.

Every year, when the first leaves began to fall, every family in the kingdom made a paper lantern. They painted it and decorated it and tucked a candle inside. And at sunset, they carried their lanterns down to the lake and set them floating on the water — one by one by one — until the whole lake glowed like a sky full of stars.

It was the most beautiful night of the year.

Princess Savannah loved the Lantern Festival more than almost anything. She loved the painting and the gluing and the glitter. She loved the walk down to the water at dusk. She loved the moment when all the lanterns drifted out and the lake lit up with gold.

This year, she had a plan.

She was going to make the most perfect lantern anyone had ever seen.

She sat at a long wooden table in the village square with jars of paint lined up in a row, sheets of colored tissue paper stacked just so, pots of glue, and three big bowls of glitter — silver, gold, and blue.

Finnegan the Fox sat across from her, carefully cutting perfect star shapes out of yellow paper. He was very proud of his scissors work. "Look at this one," he said, holding up a star. "Five points. Not a wobble in sight."

"It's lovely, Finnegan," said Savannah.

"I know," said Finnegan.

Hazel the Owl perched on the fence post nearby, watching everything with her big, calm eyes. "Do you think the lake remembers last year's lanterns?" she asked.

"I don't think lakes remember things, Hazel," said Finnegan.

"I think they might," said Hazel.

Families worked side by side at the long tables, chatting and laughing and getting glue on their sleeves. Savannah smoothed a sheet of pale blue tissue paper and began to paint tiny golden stars across it, very carefully, one by one.

And that was when Luna arrived.

Luna was a dragon — a big, black-scaled, bright-eyed dragon with a long swishy tail and a nose that was always poking into things. She was brave and smart and cuddly and loud, and she loved Savannah more than anything in the whole wide world.

And right now, she wanted to help. She wanted to help *so much.*

Luna trotted up to the table and pushed her big snout right between the paint jars. Her tail swished. Her eyes were wide and eager.

"Careful, Luna," said Savannah. "The paint —"

*Bump.*

Luna's nose nudged the blue paint jar, and it tipped over sideways and sent a river of blue paint rolling across the table, right over Savannah's tissue paper with the tiny golden stars.

"Oh no," said Savannah.

Luna looked sorry. She looked very sorry. But she also still wanted to help.

So she climbed up onto the bench to get a better look — and sat right down on a fresh sheet of tissue paper. *Crinkle, crinkle, crunch.* When she stood up again, the paper was flat and wrinkled and shaped exactly like a dragon's bottom.

"Luna!" said Savannah.

Finnegan tried to help. "Hey, Luna, want to hear a joke? What did the lantern say to the candle?"

Luna did not want to hear a joke. Luna wanted to be close to Savannah. She wanted to be part of the making.

She leaned in to sniff the glitter.

"Luna, don't —" said Savannah.

Luna sniffed.

And then Luna sneezed.

It was an enormous sneeze. A great, big, thundering dragon-sneeze that shook the table and rattled the jars and sent glitter flying everywhere — silver and gold and blue — into Savannah's hair, across Finnegan's perfect star shapes, over Hazel's feathers, and all across the village square like a sparkling snowstorm.

Finnegan blinked. He was covered in glitter from nose to tail. "Well," he said. "That happened."

Hazel shook her feathers gently. A little cloud of gold glitter drifted off her. "Quite a sneeze," she said.

Savannah looked at her lantern.

It was lopsided. It was paint-smeared. It was crinkled and glitter-crusted and patched together with extra glue where it had torn. It looked nothing like what she had planned.

She tried to smooth it. She tried to fix the torn spot. She tried to wipe away the paint with her sleeve.

But the sun was dipping toward the hills, and all around her, other families were finishing their lanterns — neat, round, beautiful lanterns with careful decorations and tidy edges.

Savannah was quiet. She wasn't angry. She wasn't angry at Luna at all.

But she was a little sad.

---

Savannah carried her lantern down to the lake's edge and sat in the grass. The water was perfectly still, like a great silver mirror. The sunset painted everything in shades of peach and rose and gold.

Other families walked past, carrying their beautiful lanterns — tall ones and round ones and ones shaped like stars and moons.

Finnegan sat down beside her.

"It's got character, Savannah," he said. "That's better than perfect."

Hazel floated down from the sky and landed softly on a nearby rock. "The lake doesn't judge lanterns," she said. "It just holds them."

They were kind. They were good friends. But Savannah still felt the gap between what she had imagined and what she held in her hands.

Then she looked up.

Luna was sitting a little way off, by herself, at the edge of the grass. Her head was low. Her tail was still. She wasn't bouncing or sniffing or nudging anything. She was just sitting there, quiet and small — which was not like Luna at all.

Luna could tell Savannah was disappointed. And now Luna felt bad, too.

That was the thing that made Savannah stand up.

She could feel a little sad about a lantern. But she was not — not for one single second — going to let her best friend feel sad on festival night.

"Come on, Luna," Savannah called. "Come here."

Luna lifted her head.

"We're going to the lake," said Savannah. "All of us. Together."

Luna's tail gave one slow wag. Then a faster one. Then she was up and trotting over, pressing her big warm head against Savannah's side.

"It's just a lantern," Savannah whispered, hugging her tight. "You're more important than any lantern."

Luna rumbled softly — the sound she made when she was happy and close and exactly where she wanted to be.

---

The whole kingdom walked down the hill together in a warm golden procession — families and critters, lanterns glowing softly, footsteps quiet on the fallen leaves.

Savannah carried her lumpy, lopsided, glitter-covered lantern. Luna walked close beside her.

Finnegan trotted along on Savannah's other side. "Hey," he said. "What did the lantern say to the lake?"

"What?" said Savannah.

"I'm *glowing* to meet you."

Savannah laughed. It felt good to laugh.

Hazel flew ahead and circled back. "The lake is beautiful tonight," she reported. "Perfectly still. Like a mirror."

They reached the water's edge and found their place among the other families. The whole shore was lined with people holding lanterns, waiting for the last light of day to fade.

And there, standing tall and lovely by the water, was Wilma.

Wilma was one of the kingdom's elders — elegant and beautiful, with kind eyes that saw everything. Her own lantern was simple and graceful, like she was.

She saw Savannah coming and smiled. She knelt beside her and looked at the messy lantern without a single word of criticism.

She ran a gentle hand over the crinkled paper, the smeared paint, the glitter that was everywhere, catching the last of the sunset light.

"This one," said Wilma, "was made with a lot of love." She looked at Savannah with those warm, knowing eyes. "You can always tell."

She helped Savannah set a small candle inside the lantern and showed her how to ease it out onto the water — gently, gently, like setting a baby bird on a branch.

"Go on," said Wilma. "It's ready."

---

Savannah knelt at the water's edge. She held the lantern in both hands. The candle flickered inside, sending a soft, warm glow through the crinkled paper.

She gave it a gentle push.

The lantern wobbled out onto the still water. It floated among all the other lanterns — the neat round ones, the tall ones, the ones shaped like stars and moons. Hers looked like a crumpled, glittery, paint-smeared mess among all of them.

Savannah watched it go.

And then the last sliver of sun dropped below the hills.

And something happened.

Savannah's lantern began to glow. Not the soft flicker of a candle — but a warm, steady, golden light from deep inside. It grew brighter and brighter, shining through every wrinkle and paint smear and patch of glitter until the whole lantern shimmered like a jewel on the water.

It glowed brighter than any other lantern on the lake.

The whole kingdom noticed. People turned to look. The golden light caught all the glitter and the scattered paint and turned every messy, lopsided bit of it into something beautiful — something that sparkled and danced across the dark water like a tiny sun.

Savannah stared. "How...?"

Finnegan's eyes went wide. "What did you put in that thing?"

Hazel tilted her head. "Interesting," she said.

Savannah turned around.

Luna was sitting behind her, watching the glowing lantern with big, proud, shining eyes. Her tail swished slowly back and forth across the grass.

And Savannah understood.

While she wasn't looking — while she was busy fixing and smoothing and worrying — Luna had leaned in close and breathed one tiny, gentle puff of dragon-fire into the lantern. Just a whisper of flame. The smallest gift she knew how to give.

Luna couldn't help with scissors. She couldn't help with paint. She couldn't keep her tail still or her nose out of the glitter.

But she could give it this.

Savannah's eyes filled up with happy tears. She threw her arms around Luna's neck and held on tight.

"You were helping the whole time," she whispered. "The whole time, Luna."

Luna rumbled — a deep, happy, proud rumble that echoed across the water.

Finnegan grinned from ear to ear. "I *knew* that lantern had something extra."

Hazel nodded slowly. "The best things," she said, "are never the ones you planned."

The other families gathered around to admire the brightest lantern on the lake, and Savannah told every one of them — "Luna made it glow."

Luna lifted her head high and let out a happy roar that rang across the water and made the other lanterns bob and dance. She was loud and proud and not one bit sorry about it.

---

The kingdom sat together on the shore and watched all the lanterns drift across the dark lake. Soft lights floating on still water, a hundred tiny stars.

And right in the middle of them all, one lantern glowed the brightest — lopsided, paint-smeared, glitter-covered, and golden as the sun.

Savannah leaned against Luna's warm side. Finnegan curled up in the grass nearby with his tail over his nose. Hazel settled on a low branch overhead and closed her eyes.

Wilma came and draped a soft blanket over Savannah's shoulders. She tucked it in, gentle and warm, and looked out at the lake.

"Same time next year," she said.

Savannah smiled. Luna rumbled. The lanterns floated on and on.

And the brightest one — the messy, lumpy, crinkled, glitter-crusted, dragon-lit one — glowed the longest of all.

And every autumn after that, when the Lantern Festival came around, Savannah never made her lantern alone. She always made it with Luna — paint spills and crinkled paper and glitter-sneezes and all.

And it was always, *always* the brightest one on the lake.

The end.
