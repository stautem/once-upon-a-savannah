# Story: The Dragon Who Loved Jigsaw Puzzles

**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Characters:** Princess Savannah, Luna, Ollie the Opossum, Mossy the Turtle, Clover the Deer
**Reading time:** ~8 minutes

---

Once upon a time, in a quiet little kingdom by a silver lake, there lived a princess named Savannah and a dragon named Luna.

Every morning, they walked down to the shore together. The lake was calm and cool, with smooth pebble beaches and wildflower meadows that stretched all the way to the village green. Savannah liked to skip stones across the water — one, two, three hops — and watch the ripples spread.

Luna liked to dig.

She dug in the shallows. She dug in the mud. She sniffed every rock on the shore as if each one had a secret to tell. Sometimes she found an especially good rock and carried it around in her mouth for the rest of the morning, very pleased with herself.

Their friends were always nearby.

Ollie the Opossum napped in a pile of leaves by the water's edge, one eye open just in case something exciting happened. Mossy the Turtle sat on his favorite warm rock, watching the clouds drift by as slowly as he did. And Clover the Deer came down to the lake to drink, quiet and graceful, her hooves barely making a sound on the pebbles.

It was the kind of morning where nothing needed to happen, and that was exactly right.

But then one night, a big rainstorm came.

It rained and rained and rained. Thunder rumbled over the lake. Lightning flickered behind the hills. The water rose and churned, and when morning came and the storm had passed, the lake had pulled back from the shore, farther than anyone could remember.

And it had uncovered something.

Luna found it first. Of course she did.

When Savannah arrived at the shore that morning, Luna was already there, knee-deep in the mud, sniffing and nudging and pawing at the ground. Hundreds of flat stone pieces lay scattered in the wet earth — all different shapes, all different sizes, covered in faded colors of blue and gold and black and green.

Luna had already arranged a dozen of them into a little cluster, pushing them together with her nose. Her tail thumped the ground. Her eyes were wide and bright. She growled softly at the pile — not an angry growl, but a thinking growl, the kind she made when she was figuring something out.

"Luna?" said Savannah. "What did you find?"

Luna didn't even look up.

Savannah had never seen her like this. Luna wouldn't leave. She wouldn't eat. She wouldn't even roar at the squirrels, and Luna always roared at the squirrels.

She had found a puzzle. And she was going to solve it.

The trouble was, the puzzle was enormous. There were hundreds and hundreds of pieces, maybe thousands, spread all along the muddy shore. Far too many for one dragon, even a very determined one.

Savannah knelt down and picked up a stone piece. It was smooth and flat, with a curve of faded blue painted on one side. She studied the cluster Luna had started, found a spot where the blue might continue, and set the piece down.

Luna's head snapped around. She looked at the piece. She looked at Savannah. And she gave a deep, rumbling growl — the kind that said, very clearly: *No. Not there.*

She nudged the piece right back out with her nose.

"Okay," said Savannah. "Sorry."

Ollie wandered over to see what the fuss was about. He picked up a piece, turned it this way and that, and placed it next to Luna's cluster with a hopeful smile.

Luna huffed at him so hard his ears blew back.

Ollie's eyes went wide. He wobbled. He swayed. And then he fell over sideways into the mud and played dead.

He stayed that way for ten minutes.

Mossy, who had been watching all of this from his warm rock, made his way over slowly. He looked at the pieces Luna had arranged. He looked at the pieces she had rejected. He tilted his head one way, then the other.

"She's not being mean," Mossy said in his slow, steady voice. "She just knows where things go. She can see it."

Savannah looked at Luna, who was already back to work, nosing another piece into place with great care and concentration.

Mossy was right. Luna wasn't being difficult. She could see the picture — or at least, she could feel where each piece belonged. She just couldn't do it all by herself.

Savannah stood up and brushed the mud off her knees.

"I have an idea," she said. "Luna can't do this alone, but she can't do it with people putting pieces in the wrong places, either. So here's what we'll do."

She looked at her friends.

"We're going to have a puzzle party."

They would carry all the pieces up to the village green, where there was room to spread them out. They would sort the pieces by color and by edge. And Luna would be the boss of where everything went.

"Every piece, every placement — Luna decides," said Savannah.

Luna looked up from her work. She tilted her head. Then she thumped her tail twice, hard, which meant: *Yes. That is exactly right. Carry on.*

And so they got to work.

It took all day to carry the stone pieces from the shore to the village green. Savannah organized teams. Villagers brought baskets. Everyone helped.

Ollie helped too — dramatically.

"It's SO heavy, Savannah," he groaned, clutching a single stone piece to his chest. "I can't POSSIBLY carry another one. This is the end. Tell my story. Remember me well."

Then he picked up three more pieces and carried them all the way to the green without stopping.

Mossy was slow, as Mossy always was. But he never dropped a single piece. He carried each one carefully, setting it down gently in the sorting area, and then turning around to go get another. Step by step. Stone by stone. That was Mossy's way.

And Clover appeared silently with a basket balanced perfectly on her back, set it down at the edge of the green without a word, and disappeared to get another. Then another. Then another. She never asked for thanks. She never made a fuss. She was just there, every time they needed her, steady as the dawn.

By afternoon, the village green looked like a giant colorful carpet. Thousands of stone pieces spread across the grass, sorted into piles — blues here, golds there, greens along the edge, blacks in the middle.

Luna paced around it all, inspecting everything. She rumbled approval at a pile of well-sorted blues. She snorted at a stack of greens that had a gold mixed in. She was in charge, and everyone knew it, and she was having the time of her life.

Savannah set up a system. The sorters would find pieces that might match, hold them up where Luna could see, and wait.

If Luna nodded — a big, slow nod with her whole head — the piece was right, and she would take it gently in her mouth and place it herself.

If Luna snorted — a sharp huff through her nostrils — the piece went back to the pile.

It became a game. The whole village got into it. People cheered when a piece fit. They groaned and laughed when Luna rejected one. Children held up pieces and called, "How about this one, Luna?" and Luna would look very seriously at each one before deciding.

Piece by piece, the mosaic grew.

First, the edges took shape — a wide, curving border of blue and green, like sky meeting treetops. Then, in the center, something began to appear. Dark shapes. A proud curve. The sweep of something wide and strong.

Scales. Wings. A long, graceful neck.

"It's an animal," said Ollie, squinting.

"It's bigger than an animal," said Savannah.

Mossy, who had been studying the stones very quietly all afternoon, spoke up.

"These stones are very, very old," he said. "Older than the village. Older than anyone here remembers."

Clover, who had been gathering pieces near the edge of the mosaic, nudged one toward Savannah with her nose. It was different from the others — along its border, tiny words had been carved into the stone, worn almost smooth by time and water.

Savannah knelt down and traced the letters with her finger.

"For the first friend," she read aloud.

The village went quiet.

Luna kept working. She didn't stop. She didn't slow down. Piece by piece, the picture grew clearer — a dragon, black-scaled and bright-eyed, with wings spread wide and a look on its face that was brave and gentle all at once.

The very last piece sat on the grass in front of Luna. She looked at it for a long moment. Then she picked it up, very gently, in her mouth. She carried it to the center of the mosaic — the very heart of the picture — and set it down.

It fit perfectly.

The mosaic was complete.

Everyone stepped back.

And there it was.

A dragon. Black scales shining with flecks of gold. Bright, knowing eyes. Wings stretched wide as if ready to fly. A face that was fierce and kind at the same time — the face of someone who was brave enough to be gentle.

She looked exactly like Luna.

The village was still. The evening light fell golden across the green, and the mosaic glowed as if it had been waiting all those long, buried years for this exact moment.

This picture had been made long, long ago — a monument to the very first dragon who had ever befriended the kingdom. And that dragon, painted in stone before anyone alive could remember, looked just like the one standing over her portrait right now.

Luna stared at the mosaic for a long time.

Then, slowly, she lay down right beside it. She rested her big, warm head against the stone dragon's nose, so gently, as if she were greeting an old friend.

And she made that sound — the deep, low rumble that meant she was perfectly, completely happy.

Savannah sat down beside her. She rested her hand on Luna's neck, feeling the warmth of her scales and the steady rhythm of her breathing.

Ollie curled up against Luna's side, tucking himself in tight. Clover settled in the grass nearby, folding her legs beneath her. Mossy found a warm spot at the edge of the mosaic and pulled his head partway into his shell, content.

"She knew," Mossy said quietly. "The whole time, she knew it was hers."

Savannah smiled and leaned her head against Luna's shoulder. "Of course she did."

The village decided, right then and there, to keep the mosaic on the green forever. They would build a low stone wall around it to protect it, and set little lanterns at its corners, and everyone would know the story — how a dragon named Luna had found the kingdom's oldest treasure, buried by the lake, waiting for her all along.

That evening, the whole village gathered on the green under the first stars. Someone brought blankets. Someone brought tea. The fireflies came out over the lake, blinking their tiny lights like a second sky.

And in the middle of it all, sprawled across the ancient stone portrait of the kingdom's very first dragon friend, Luna slept.

Her tail curled around a pile of leftover puzzle pieces she'd decided to keep, just in case. Her breath came slow and warm. The mosaic beneath her glowed faintly in the starlight, as if the old stone dragon was happy to finally have company.

Savannah leaned against her, warm and close, and watched the stars come out one by one.

And if you visit that little kingdom by the silver lake today, you can still see the mosaic on the village green. You can still read the words carved along the edge — *For the first friend.* And you can still find a black dragon sleeping right on top of it, every single night, right where she belongs.

And they all lived happily ever after.
