# Luna in the Fog

**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Characters:** Princess Savannah, Luna, Big Al, Bramble the Bat, Ollie the Opossum, The Fireflies
**Reading time:** ~8 minutes

---

Once upon a time, in a little village at the edge of a silver lake, there lived a princess named Savannah.

Princess Savannah had a best friend, and her best friend was a dragon.

The dragon's name was Luna. She was big and black-scaled and shining, with wings like velvet and a tail that knocked over fence posts when she wagged it. She was brave and smart and cuddly and very, very loud.

Every morning, Savannah and Luna walked down to the lake together. Luna would splash in the shallows and roar at the ducks. The ducks would look at her, shake their feathers, and paddle away slowly, because ducks are not easily impressed.

Every afternoon, they explored the old woods behind the village, finding mushrooms and mossy rocks and little streams that sang over the stones.

And every evening, they sat together on the dock and watched the sun sink into the silver water, and Luna would rest her big dark head in Savannah's lap, and Savannah would scratch behind her ears, and Luna would purr — a deep, rumbling sound, like thunder that had decided to be gentle.

It was a very good life.

But one evening, something changed.

A fog rolled in off the lake.

Not a thin, wispy fog. A thick, soft, silver fog — the kind that swallows up the world and makes everything quiet. It crept over the water and through the reeds and between the houses and up into the trees, until Savannah couldn't see the lake anymore. She couldn't see the dock. She couldn't even see her own garden gate.

"It'll lift by morning," she told herself, and went to sleep.

But when morning came, the fog was still there. Thicker now. Softer. So quiet that Savannah could hear her own heartbeat.

She opened the door and called out: "Luna!"

No answer.

She called again: "Luna! Come here, girl!"

And then — from somewhere far away, muffled by the fog — she heard it.

A roar.

Then a whimper.

Then another roar.

Luna was out there. Somewhere in the fog. And she sounded upset.

Savannah pulled on her boots. She was going to find her.

But first, she went to see Big Al.

Big Al lived in a stone cottage by the old bridge, where the moss grew thick on the walls and the chimney always had smoke curling from it. He was the biggest man in the village — broad as a barn door, strong as the bridge itself. He knew things about the lake and the fog and the woods that nobody else remembered.

"A fog like this," said Big Al, leaning in his doorway and looking out at the white, "it comes and goes. Won't last forever. But Luna won't know that." He scratched his chin. "She'll be scared because she can't see you. That's the hardest kind of scared — when you can't find the person you love."

He reached inside and brought out a horn, carved from pale wood, smooth and curved like a seashell.

"Blow this as you go," he said. "So she can hear you coming. Sometimes knowing someone's on their way is enough."

Savannah hugged him — Big Al's hugs always made her feel like nothing in the world could go wrong — and set off into the fog.

She blew the horn.

It made a sound like the wind singing through a hollow tree — low, warm, and steady. It carried through the fog the way a lantern carries through the dark.

She blew it again and walked toward the lake.

The path was hard to follow. Everything looked the same — white and soft and still. The trees were shapes without edges. The ground was damp beneath her boots.

But then she heard a voice. Small. Upside-down.

"Oh, good. Someone with a plan."

It was Bramble the Bat, hanging from a low branch by the path. She was soft and dark, with bright little eyes and ears that turned this way and that, catching every sound.

"I heard Luna roaring half the night," said Bramble. "I can find her. I don't need to see — I listen." She tilted her ears toward the fog. "This way. Follow me."

Bramble flew ahead, calling back with little clicks and chirps that bounced off the trees and told her exactly where everything was.

Savannah blew the horn and followed.

A little farther down the path, they found Ollie the Opossum.

Ollie was curled up under a large mushroom, perfectly still, eyes shut tight.

"Ollie," said Savannah. "Are you playing dead?"

One eye opened. "Maybe," he whispered.

"The fog spooked you?"

"The fog is very spooky," he said, matter-of-factly.

"Want to come with us? We're finding Luna."

Ollie opened both eyes. He looked at the fog. He looked at Savannah. He looked at the fog again.

"I'll come," he said, very quietly. "But I'm staying close."

"That's the whole point," said Savannah.

So the three of them went on together — Bramble flying ahead, clicking and calling, Savannah blowing the horn, and Ollie walking so close behind Savannah that he kept stepping on her heels.

"Sorry," said Ollie.

"It's okay," said Savannah.

"Sorry," said Ollie again, stepping on her other heel.

They followed Luna's roars. Through the fog, past the old dock, along the lakeshore where the reeds grew tall. The roars got louder. Closer. And between the roars, they could hear something else — soft little sounds. Peeping sounds.

"That's not Luna," said Bramble, tilting her ears.

"No," said Savannah. "That's something smaller."

They came around a bend in the reeds, and there she was.

Luna.

She was sitting at the very edge of the water, her big dark body blocking the wind, her wings spread wide like a tent. Fog swirled around her. Her scales glistened with tiny droplets of water.

And tucked underneath her, sheltered by her wings, were six baby ducks.

Six tiny, fluffy, peeping baby ducks, huddled together in the warmth of a dragon.

Luna looked up at Savannah. Her eyes were big and wet. She let out a low, rumbling sound — not a roar. A plea.

*Help.*

"Oh, Luna," said Savannah softly.

She understood now. Luna wasn't lost. Luna had found the ducklings in the fog. They were separated from their mother. Cold and scared and alone. And Luna — brave, loud, stubborn Luna — had stayed with them. She'd curled around them to keep them warm. She'd roared to call for help. She'd roared to scare away anything that might come too close.

She hadn't left because she couldn't leave them.

"You did so good," Savannah whispered, kneeling down and pressing her forehead to Luna's big, warm nose. "You did so, so good."

Luna purred.

"But we still need to find their mama," said Savannah. She looked out over the lake. The fog lay thick on the water, white and still. Somewhere out there, a mother duck was looking for her babies.

"I can't see anything out there," said Bramble.

"Neither can I," said Savannah. "But maybe we don't need to see."

She knelt by the reeds at the water's edge and called out, gently: "Hello? Fireflies? We could use a little light."

For a moment, nothing happened.

Then — one by one, like tiny stars deciding to wake up — the Fireflies rose from the reeds.

First one. Then three. Then a dozen. Then more than Savannah could count. They lifted out of the tall grass and drifted over the water, glowing soft and gold, until the surface of the lake shimmered with a hundred little lanterns.

They spread out across the fog in a long, winding trail of light — from the reeds where Savannah stood, all the way out over the still, silver water.

A path. A golden, glowing path.

And from somewhere out in the fog, there came a sound.

*Quack.*

Then again, closer: *Quack, quack, quack.*

The mother duck came paddling out of the white, following the trail of Fireflies, swimming fast, her head turning this way and that. She quacked and quacked and the baby ducks peeped and peeped. And when she reached the shore, the six little fluffballs tumbled out from under Luna's wings and waddled straight to their mother in a noisy, peeping pile.

Ollie wiped his eyes. "I'm not crying," he said. "My face is just wet from the fog."

"Mine too," said Bramble, who was definitely also crying.

Luna let out a long, deep breath. Her wings folded down. Her whole body relaxed, like she'd been holding herself tight for hours and could finally let go. She leaned into Savannah — all of her, all at once, which was a lot of dragon — and purred so deeply that the reeds hummed along.

Savannah wrapped her arms as far around Luna's neck as they would go, which wasn't very far, but Luna didn't mind.

"Let's go home," said Savannah.

The fog began to lift as they walked back along the lakeshore. First just a little — the trees finding their edges again, the path growing solid under their feet. Then more, the silver burning off in the morning sun, until the lake sparkled and the village rooftops appeared one by one, like old friends waving hello.

They walked in a line. Bramble rode on Luna's head, her little ears turning happily in the breeze. Ollie walked beside Savannah and only stepped on her heels twice. Luna walked behind them all, her tail swishing back and forth, knocking over exactly one fence post on the way home.

When they passed the old bridge, Big Al was standing outside his cottage, arms folded, a knowing smile on his face.

"Found her, did you?"

"She wasn't really lost," said Savannah. "She was being brave."

Big Al looked at Luna. Luna looked at Big Al. She let out a roar — not a scared roar or a help roar, but a proud, happy, I'm-home roar.

Big Al chuckled. "That's a good dragon."

That evening, after the fog was gone and the lake was silver-blue again, Savannah and Luna sat on the dock together, like they always did. The ducks paddled by — the mother and all six babies in a little line. Luna roared at them, just once, because that was what she did.

The ducks were not impressed.

But the smallest duckling looked back and peeped — just once — and Luna's tail wagged so hard it splashed water all the way up onto the dock.

Savannah laughed, and Luna purred, and the Fireflies blinked on in the reeds, and the stars came out one by one over the silver lake.

And they all lived happily ever after.
