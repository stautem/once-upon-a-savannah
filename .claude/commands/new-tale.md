# /new-tale — Generate a New Fairy Tale

You are creating a new bedtime fairy tale for the once-upon-a-savannah project.

## What to do

1. **Get the premise.** If `$ARGUMENTS` is provided, use it as the story premise or seed idea (e.g., "Luna accidentally freezes the garden" or "Savannah finds a lost baby owl"). Otherwise, ask:
   - "What's the adventure about? Even one sentence is enough — 'Luna gets lost in the fog' or 'the kingdom's flowers won't bloom.'"

2. **Read the project rules:**
   - Read `CLAUDE.md` for voice, tone, and story structure rules
   - Read `characters.md` for the character bible
   - Read `templates/fairy-tale.md` for the story template

3. **Create the outline first:**
   - Pick a short, hyphenated name for the story (e.g., `frozen-garden`, `lost-little-owl`)
   - Create `stories/{name}/outline.md` — brief beat-by-beat outline following the story structure in `CLAUDE.md`
   - Include which characters and critters will appear
   - **Present the outline to the user and ask for approval before writing the full draft.** This is the checkpoint — let them steer before 2,000 words are on the page. They may want to swap characters, adjust the twist, or change direction entirely.

4. **Once approved, write the full story** in `stories/{name}/draft.md` following these rules:
   - 1,000-2,000 words. Readable aloud in 5-10 minutes.
   - Classic fairy tale cadence. "Once upon a time..." opening. "Happily ever after" closing.
   - Princess Savannah is the hero. She solves the problem through kindness and friendship.
   - Luna appears as a dragon — she can be the mystery, the sidekick, or just part of the crew from the start. She doesn't have to be the twist every time.
   - Big Al and/or Wilma can appear as wise helpers if the story calls for them.
   - Pick 2-4 woodland critters that fit the adventure (see `characters.md` for the palette). No fixed crew — mix and match freely.
   - Gentle humor is welcome. Nothing scary. Nothing sad. Pure comfort.
   - Use repetition and rhythm — repeated phrases, patterns of three, call-and-response.
   - Read every sentence aloud in your head. If it stumbles, simplify.
   - The ending must be warm, safe, and happy. No ambiguity.

5. **After writing, report:**
   - Story title and location
   - Estimated reading time
   - A one-line summary
   - Ask if the author wants any changes

## Rules
- This is a gift for someone who misses her grandparents' bedtime stories. Every word should serve that warmth.
- Don't add moral lessons unless they emerge naturally.
- Don't make it scary. The "problem" is always manageable.
- Don't over-complicate. Simple is better. Repeatable is better.
- No food guilt, body shaming, or any toxic messaging. Characters eat and enjoy food freely. Only build kids up, never tear them down.
- These stories will be read aloud at bedtime. The rhythm matters more than the vocabulary.
